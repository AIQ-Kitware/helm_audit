\section{Executive Summary}
\label{sec:tr-summary}
% =====================================================================

This technical report documents an end-to-end reproducibility audit of
  three open-weight model families
  (Pythia-6.9B, Vicuna-7B v1.3, Falcon-7B) against public HELM run
  artifacts from the relevant HELM suites, including HELM Classic
  where applicable, on fourteen single-turn benchmarks, totalling
  42 \mbox{(model, benchmark)} cells.
The audit operates entirely through the \eee{} schema and is
  empirically validated --- via a runtime audit hook installed into
  every child Python process --- to read zero HELM run-dir artifacts
  during analysis (88 child processes, 205{,}798 file-open events,
  6{,}902 unique paths, 0 flagged).

\paragraph{Headline numbers.}
On the resulting heatmap, 39 of 42 cells have a micro-averaged
  instance-level agree\_ratio in $[0.788, 1.000]$ at \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9};
  the remaining 3 are the \texttt{entity\_matching} cells across all
  three models, where official-side and local-side \texttt{sample\_hash}
  sets have zero overlap.

\paragraph{Three reproducibility-disagreement mechanisms identified.}
Each illustrates a different layer at which two runs of nominally
  the same recipe can disagree.
Two of the three (Cases~A and~B below) are upstream issues that we
  attribute to the HELM serving and data-pipeline stack;
  the third (Case~C) is intentional recipe-level stochasticity that
  the benchmark explicitly enables and that any honest audit must
  treat as the design-baked floor, not as a reproducibility bug.
EEE's role differs across the three.
Case~A is detected directly by EEE's content-derived
  \texttt{sample\_hash}
  (a SHA-256 hash over the rendered prompt and the first correct
  reference, computed at conversion time; see
  Section~\ref{sec:tr-eee-format}): the hash sets fail to overlap
  between sides, so the join itself rejects the comparison and the
  cell renders as \texttt{join\_failed}.
HELM's positional \texttt{id<i>}-based comparison, in contrast, would
  silently agree on Case~A's mismatched examples.
Cases~B and~C, however, do \emph{not} fail at the hash-join layer ---
  the prompts and references are byte-equivalent on both sides, so
  the hashes match and the join succeeds.
They are surfaced \emph{after} alignment: Case~B via
  \texttt{evaluation.score} divergence per (sample, metric) row,
  combined with the empty \texttt{output.raw} pattern on the official
  side, and Case~C via the run-level mean accuracy gap and
  cross-model uniformity diagnostic on the joined data
  (\texttt{evaluation.score} variance under the recipe's stochastic
  sampling).
\eee{} preserves the data needed for both forms of detection ---
  prompt and reference for the hash-join, and raw output and per-metric
  score for the post-join score comparison --- but the hash is
  load-bearing only for Case~A.
The hash validates whether two records describe the same
  prompt-reference pair; it does not retrieve or address the
  underlying content.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Data-pipeline non-determinism (Case~A).}
        The \texttt{entity\_matching} \texttt{join\_failed} cells are
        caused by \texttt{pd.merge} producing different row orderings
        between pandas~2.0.x (HELM Classic v0.3.0 era) and pandas~2.2+
        (current) on byte-identical input CSVs.
        HELM's downstream \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} assigns
        identifiers positionally over the merged list, so the same
        \texttt{id<i>} binds to different rows under different pandas
        versions.
        We close this loop empirically: we reproduce the official
        v0.3.0 \texttt{scenario\_state.json} byte-for-byte from a
        \texttt{crfm-helm==0.3.0 + pandas==2.0.3 + numpy==1.23.5} venv,
        and our local audit \texttt{scenario\_state.json} byte-for-byte
        from a current venv (\texttt{pandas==2.3.3 + numpy==2.2.6}).
  \item \textbf{Inference-backend non-determinism (Case~B).}
        The \texttt{synthetic\_reasoning\_natural~$\times$~Pythia-6.9B}
        cell at $0.788$ is a Pythia-specific artifact of HELM's
        deployment routing on the official side: with
        \texttt{model\_deployment=None}, HELM v0.3.0's
        \texttt{auto\_client.py} dispatches \texttt{eleutherai/*}
        models to Together.ai's hosted API, where Pythia at
        \texttt{temperature=0} emits a leading
        \texttt{`\textbackslash n'} as its greedy first token.
        HELM's \texttt{stop\_sequences=[`\textbackslash n']} then
        trims \texttt{completion.text} to empty before scoring, and
        the resulting HELM-published score on the Stanford dashboard
        is $0\%$ accuracy on every one of the 1{,}000 evaluation
        instances.
        Our local re-run, served via HuggingFace transformers,
        produces a greedy first token of \texttt{`The'}, captures the
        model's actual answer, and recovers $\approx 20\%$ accuracy on
        the same task.
  \item \textbf{Recipe-level stochasticity (Case~C).}
        The \texttt{wikifact} cells at $\approx 0.92$ across all three
        models reflect HELM's recipe-level non-determinism: the
        benchmark pins \texttt{temperature=1.0, num\_outputs=5}, and
        the expected agreement of two independent stochastic runs on
        a benchmark with per-prompt \texttt{exact\_match@5} hit rate
        $p_i$ decomposes as
        $\mathbb{E}_i[p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2]
        = \text{agree}(\bar p) + 2\,\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i)$
        (formalized in
        \texttt{wikifact\_consistency\_claim.lean}, theorem
        \texttt{expectedAgreement\_eq\_uniformAgreement\_add\_variance}).
        This is the only one of the three mechanisms not surfaceable as
        a reproducibility bug; it is the design-baked floor that any
        honest reproducibility audit must acknowledge.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The systematic Pythia-Together-vs-HuggingFace pattern.}
Eleven cells fall below 0.98 agreement on the slim heatmap; ten of
  them are Pythia-6.9B.
The non-WikiFact eight Pythia cells share a single underlying
  mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:tr-pythia-pattern}): at
  \texttt{temperature=0}, Together-hosted Pythia and HuggingFace-served
  Pythia disagree at the greedy argmax for the first divergent token
  on a small fraction of prompts.
SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia is the catastrophic special case where the
  divergence happens at the very first generated token AND the
  divergent token is \texttt{`\textbackslash n'} (the
  \texttt{stop\_sequence}), zeroing the score outright; the other
  Pythia cells are the same backend divergence under benchmarks whose
  scorers tolerate it more gracefully.
Vicuna-7B v1.3 and Falcon-7B --- also Together-hosted in the official
  run --- agree at $\geq 0.984$ on every non-WikiFact cell, indicating
  the divergence is Pythia-specific and consistent with a logit-margin
  effect from Pythia's smaller training-compute budget and absence of
  instruction tuning (Section~\ref{sec:tr-pythia-pattern}).

\paragraph{Three failure modes of micro-averaged agree\_ratio.}
A reproducibility report that surfaces the cell-level number alone
  cannot distinguish a recipe-clean near-perfect reproduction from
  three alternative regimes that the audit catalogues
  (Section~\ref{sec:tr-microavg}):
  (i)~\emph{degenerate-zero agreement}, where one side scores 0 on
  every row and the agreement reads as ``fraction the other side also
  failed'' (SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia at $0.788$);
  (ii)~\emph{both-wrong masking}, where outputs differ on a fraction of
  prompts but the metric scores both as 0, so the agreement reads as
  1.0 even though outputs differ (Falcon~$\times$~SR at $0.999$ hides
  a $1.0\%$ true output-divergence rate);
  (iii)~\emph{stochastic noise floor}, where recipe-pinned
  \texttt{temperature>0} sets an inherent non-zero disagreement
  expectation regardless of any reproduction effort
  (\texttt{wikifact} at $\approx 0.92$).

\paragraph{EEE audit/forensics scope.}
Each of the three case studies was \emph{detected} from EEE alone but
  required walking back to HELM's run-time artifacts
  (\texttt{run\_spec.json}, \texttt{scenario\_state.json}, and the
  framework source) to identify the proximate \emph{mechanism}.
This is a deliberate scope decision (Section~\ref{sec:tr-scope-split}):
  EEE preserves enough for audit, deliberately less for forensics.

\paragraph{Tooling delivered.}
We release four reproducer tools alongside this report
  (Section~\ref{sec:tr-tooling}):
  \texttt{audit\_eee\_only\_run.py} (runtime EEE-only paper-validity
  hook),
  \texttt{em\_pandas\_mwe.sh} (5-combo pandas-merge isolation
  reproducer),
  \texttt{em\_helm\_mwe.sh} (end-to-end HELM-in-the-loop reproducer
  for Case~A),
  and \texttt{diagnose\_entity\_matching\_join.py} (Q1--Q5
  divergence-pattern diagnostic).
We also contribute four code changes to the audit pipeline:
  registration of the \texttt{*\_set\_match} metric family in the core
  classifier, a new \texttt{no\_core\_metrics} heatmap status
  (distinct from \texttt{join\_failed}), automatic emission of a
  per-output-dir \texttt{redraw\_plots.sh} alongside the rendered
  heatmap, and a tightened heatmap colour scale and dynamic legend.

\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:tr-background}
% =====================================================================

Evaluation reports for large language models are increasingly relied
  on as evidence in research papers, leaderboards, model cards, and
  governance assessments.
A published HELM run records, for each evaluation instance, a
  per-metric score that a downstream consumer can read directly off
  the leaderboard.
What the leaderboard does \emph{not} record on a per-record basis is
  the upstream-framework version, the inference-backend identity, or
  the recipe-level stochasticity parameters that determine whether
  two runs of nominally the same recipe should agree.

The reproducibility question for any single such record is:

\begin{quote}\itshape
For a published HELM evaluation result, can a downstream consumer who
  has only the published artifacts (a)~detect whether a re-execution
  agrees with the published number, (b)~localize where any
  disagreement comes from, and (c)~reach a defensible verdict about
  whether the disagreement reflects a real reproducibility failure or
  an expected artifact of the evaluation recipe?
\end{quote}

The audit's first job is to \emph{distinguish} layers of disagreement,
  not just to count instance-level mismatches.
A mismatch that comes from \texttt{temperature=1} stochastic sampling
  is a different finding from a mismatch that comes from a silent
  pandas-version-dependent reshuffle of the test set.
Both are present in the slim-paper grid we audit; both are detected
  by \eee{}; the mechanisms are completely different.

\subsection{Artifact provenance: who produced what}
\label{sec:tr-artifact-provenance}

The audit compares two sides per \mbox{(model, benchmark)} cell.
What we did, and what we relied on others to have done, matters for
  every claim in this report; we lay it out explicitly here.

\paragraph{Public-side HELM artifacts (CRFM-published, not ours).}
The official side of each comparison is anchored in public HELM run
  artifacts published by Stanford CRFM, drawn from the relevant
  HELM suite for that benchmark, including HELM Classic v0.3.0 where
  applicable.
We did not run those official evaluations; CRFM did, on whatever
  hardware, serving stack, and HELM version they used at publication
  time.
We mirror those public run dirs locally and treat their contents
  (\texttt{run\_spec.json}, \texttt{scenario\_state.json},
  \texttt{stats.json}, \texttt{per\_instance\_stats.json}) as the
  authoritative published record.
For the slim heatmap exercised in this report, every cell happens to
  draw from \texttt{classic/v0.3.0/}, but the audit pipeline is
  suite-agnostic: the public-store namespace also contains
  \texttt{lite/}, \texttt{mmlu/}, \texttt{capabilities/},
  \texttt{long-context/}, \texttt{safety/}, \texttt{medhelm/},
  \texttt{finance/}, \texttt{heim/}, \texttt{image2struct/},
  \texttt{ewok/}, \texttt{speech/}, and other suites at their own
  released versions, all of which the same audit pipeline can
  consume.

\paragraph{Public-side EEE conversion (ours).}
We then converted those public HELM run dirs into \eee{} artifacts
  using our \texttt{every\_eval\_ever.converters.helm} converter
  (schema version \texttt{0.2.2}), one EEE record set per
  HELM run dir.
The converted public-side artifacts are stored under
  \path{/data/crfm-helm-audit-store/crfm-helm-public-eee-test/<suite>/<version>/...},
  with \texttt{classic/v0.3.0/} the dominant suite/version for the
  slim heatmap but not the only public-store namespace.
Every aggregate JSON in that tree records
  \texttt{source\_metadata.evaluator\_relationship="third\_party"}
  and \texttt{source\_metadata.source\_organization\_name="CRFM"} to
  mark the underlying evaluation as published-by-CRFM but
  EEE-converted-by-us.

\paragraph{Local-side HELM runs (ours).}
For each \mbox{(model, benchmark)} pair on the slim grid we re-ran
  HELM ourselves under the matching recipe.
Pythia-6.9B and Vicuna-7B v1.3 runs were performed under the
  \texttt{audit-historic-grid} experiment on
  \texttt{aiq-gpu} between 2026-03-28 and 2026-03-29;
  Falcon-7B runs were performed under the
  \texttt{audit-falcon-7b-helm-grid} experiment on the same host
  on 2026-04-30.
Both experiments used HELM version \texttt{crfm-helm} 0.5.x
  (specifically the head of the \texttt{submodules/helm/} checkout
  in this repository at the time of run, dated 2026-04-03; see
  \texttt{provenance.json} sibling files in each EEE conversion
  for exact per-run timestamps).
Local HELM run dirs are at
  \path{/data/crfm-helm-audit/<experiment_name>/helm/helm_id_<id>/benchmark_output/runs/<experiment_name>/<run_spec>/}.

\paragraph{Local-side EEE conversion (ours).}
We converted our own HELM run dirs into \eee{} artifacts with the
  same \texttt{every\_eval\_ever.converters.helm} converter (same
  version) used on the public side, so the two sides go through the
  same conversion code path.
Local EEE artifacts are at
  \path{/data/crfm-helm-audit-store/eee/local/<experiment_name>/helm_id_<id>/<run_slug>/eee_output/};
  each conversion is reproducible from a sidecar \texttt{reproduce.sh}
  alongside its \texttt{provenance.json}.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
This audit's claims are claims about the \emph{four} artifact sets
  above (public HELM run dirs, public EEE conversion, local HELM run
  dirs, local EEE conversion).
We do not have access to whatever Together.ai-side serving
  configuration produced the public HELM completions
  (relevant to Case~B; see Section~\ref{sec:tr-case-b}); we have only
  the resulting completion text and tokens that CRFM committed to the
  public run dirs.
The audit therefore reasons about \emph{output behaviour} of
  Together-served Pythia (visible in CRFM's
  \texttt{scenario\_state.json}) versus HuggingFace-served Pythia
  (visible in our local \texttt{scenario\_state.json}); it does not
  reason about Together's internal serving stack.

\section{Audit Pipeline Architecture}
\label{sec:tr-pipeline}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{The \eee{} artifact format}
\label{sec:tr-eee-format}

The audit consumes \eee{} artifacts on both sides of the comparison.
For each evaluation run, \eee{} stores:
\begin{itemize}
  \item An aggregate JSON file with run-level metadata
        (\texttt{schema\_version}, \texttt{evaluation\_id},
        \texttt{model\_id}, \texttt{evaluation\_name},
        \texttt{evaluation\_timestamp}, \texttt{source\_metadata},
        \texttt{eval\_library},
        \texttt{evaluation\_results}).
  \item A companion \texttt{<uuid>\_samples.jsonl} file with one
        record per (instance, metric), each containing
        \texttt{input.raw} (the prompt the model saw),
        \texttt{output.raw} (the model's completions),
        \texttt{answer\_attribution.extracted\_value} (what the
        evaluator extracted),
        \texttt{evaluation.score} (the per-metric score the harness
        recorded),
        \texttt{sample\_id} (the harness's per-record identifier),
        and \texttt{sample\_hash} (a content-derived hash we compute
        at conversion time; the formula is given below).
\end{itemize}

The \texttt{sample\_hash} is the central identity primitive on which
  the audit depends.
For HELM-converted records, the converter at
  \texttt{every\_eval\_ever/converters/helm/instance\_level\_adapter.py:231--233}
  computes it as
\begin{lstlisting}[style=python, caption={\eee{} HELM converter
  \texttt{sample\_hash} formula (excerpt; verbatim from
  \texttt{instance\_level\_adapter.py:231--233}).\label{lst:tr-sample-hash}}]
sample_hash=sha256_string(
    state.request.prompt + (correct_refs[0] if correct_refs else '')
)
\end{lstlisting}
where \texttt{state} is HELM's per-instance \texttt{request\_state},
  \texttt{state.request.prompt} is the rendered prompt the model saw
  (instructions plus any fewshot exemplars plus the test instance),
  and \texttt{correct\_refs[0]} is the first gold reference for the
  instance.
The hash is content-derived over the prompt and first reference:
  two records with identical \texttt{request.prompt} and identical
  first \texttt{correct\_ref} produce identical hashes; any drift in
  prompt construction (a different fewshot exemplar, a different
  newline pattern, a different gold reference) produces a different
  hash.
We use the hash for join-key purposes only --- to validate that two
  records describe the same prompt-reference pair.
It does not retrieve or address the underlying content; a record's
  prompt and reference fields are the addressable surface, and the
  hash is the validation primitive over them.

For a comparison of two evaluations of nominally the same recipe
  (e.g., the published HELM run and our local re-run), the audit
  joins the two sample sets on
  \texttt{(sample\_hash, evaluation\_result\_id)} pairs.
A pair is produced if and only if both sides have the same content
  hash for the same metric; an audit that observes zero joined pairs
  is observing two evaluations whose underlying contents differ.

\subsection{Pipeline stages}
\label{sec:tr-pipeline-stages}

The slim-paper pipeline runs three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \texttt{10\_link\_tree.sh}.
        Walk the \eee{} artifacts on both sides and build a symlink
        tree under
        \texttt{eee\_artifacts/\{official,local\}/<benchmark>/<org>/<model>/}
        so a single reader can walk both sides as an \eee{}-shaped
        directory hierarchy.
        This stage is filesystem plumbing; no analysis happens here.
  \item \texttt{20\_run.sh}.
        Invoke \texttt{eval-audit-from-eee} (the EEE-only entry point
        of the analysis pipeline) over the link tree.
        For each \mbox{(model, benchmark)} cell, this produces a
        \texttt{core\_metric\_report.json} containing the
        instance-level agreement curves, per-metric breakdowns, and
        run-level mean scores.
        This is the analysis step where any reproducibility signal is
        actually computed.
  \item \texttt{30\_heatmap.sh}.
        Aggregate the per-cell reports into the publication heatmap
        (\texttt{reproducibility\_heatmap.png}) and a per-metric
        drill-down panel (one PNG per metric, under
        \texttt{reproducibility\_heatmap\_per\_metric/}).
\end{enumerate}

The output dir for the slim-paper run is at
  \path{/data/crfm-helm-audit-store/eee-only-reproducibility-heatmap-paper-slim/}.

\subsection{Runtime EEE-only paper-validity guard}
\label{sec:tr-paper-validity-guard}

A reviewer concerned with the \eee{}-sufficiency claim of this audit
  will reasonably ask: when the audit computes the published agreement
  numbers, does the analysis code actually use only EEE artifacts, or
  does it silently fall back to HELM run-dir reads when those happen
  to be on disk?
We resolve this question two ways.

\paragraph{Behaviour-level guarantee: \texttt{EVAL\_AUDIT\_EEE\_STRICT=1}.}
The \eee{} artifact loader at
  \texttt{eval\_audit/normalized/loaders.py} contains a legacy
  fallback: when the EEE artifact's provenance refers to a HELM run
  dir that exists on disk, the loader replaces the EEE-derived
  per-instance records with records read directly from
  \texttt{per\_instance\_stats.json}.
This fallback was added to support older HELM-shape consumers; it is
  a paper-validity hazard for a strict EEE-only run because it means
  the same EEE artifact, run with the same code, can produce
  different numbers depending on whether the corresponding HELM run
  dir is on the analysis host.
Setting \texttt{EVAL\_AUDIT\_EEE\_STRICT=1} disables the fallback;
  with it set, the EEE loader is provably EEE-only at the
  instance-record level.

\paragraph{Runtime-level evidence: \texttt{audit\_eee\_only\_run.py}.}
The behaviour-level guarantee is necessary but not sufficient ---
  \texttt{EVAL\_AUDIT\_EEE\_STRICT} closes the documented fallback,
  but a reviewer would reasonably want runtime evidence that no other
  HELM-shaped read sneaks through.
We implement that evidence as a wrapper script
  (\texttt{dev/oneoff/audit\_eee\_only\_run.py}) that:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Builds a temporary directory containing a
        \texttt{sitecustomize.py} that installs a
        \texttt{sys.addaudithook} on Python startup.
        The hook records every \texttt{open} and \texttt{os.open}
        event to a per-PID JSONL log file at
        \texttt{\$EVAL\_AUDIT\_AUDIT\_LOG\_DIR/audit\_<pid>.jsonl}.
  \item Prepends the temporary directory to \texttt{PYTHONPATH} and
        sets \texttt{EVAL\_AUDIT\_AUDIT\_LOG\_DIR=<log\_dir>}, so the
        sitecustomize is auto-imported by every child Python process
        that the wrapped command spawns.
        Because \texttt{eval-audit-from-eee} spawns a per-packet
        \texttt{python -m eval\_audit.reports.core\_metrics} child for
        each \mbox{(model, benchmark)} pair, the audit hook propagates
        through every analysis subprocess.
  \item Executes the wrapped command (\texttt{20\_run.sh}) under the
        instrumented environment.
  \item After the wrapped command exits, walks the log directory,
        classifies every recorded path against a HELM-shape signature
        (basenames \texttt{run\_spec.json},
        \texttt{scenario.json}, \texttt{scenario\_state.json},
        \texttt{stats.json}, \texttt{per\_instance\_stats.json};
        substring \texttt{benchmark\_output/runs/}), and writes a
        per-run report
        (\texttt{audit\_eee\_only\_run.report.json}) summarizing
        total opens, unique paths, and any flagged paths.
  \item Returns nonzero exit if any HELM-shaped path was opened.
\end{enumerate}

The hook is reentrancy-safe (a thread-local guard suppresses the
  recursive event triggered by opening the log file itself) and
  never raises out of audit-event delivery.
The full implementation is at
  \texttt{dev/oneoff/audit\_eee\_only\_run.py}; the verdict for our
  slim-paper run is reproduced in
  Table~\ref{tab:tr-audit-verdict}.

The hook catches every Python-level file open (\texttt{open},
  \texttt{pathlib.Path.open}, \texttt{json.load(file)},
  \texttt{Path.read\_text}, etc.) and every \texttt{os.open} call.
It does not catch reads from C extensions that bypass the Python audit
  framework (e.g., a C extension calling \texttt{fopen()} without
  routing through CPython's IO layer).
For belt-and-suspenders verification against this case, the same
  command can be re-wrapped under
  \texttt{strace -fe trace=openat -o trace.log} and the resulting
  syscall log grepped for the same patterns.
We did not run the strace verification for this technical report;
  the in-Python audit hook produces zero flagged events on its own,
  and we have no positive indication that a C-extension bypass
  exists in the audit code path.

\section{Scope and Methodology}
\label{sec:tr-scope}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Three-model slim slice}
\label{sec:tr-slim-models}

We picked three open-weight model families that span useful variation
  in pretraining budget, post-pretraining alignment style, and
  inference-backend default:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{eleutherai/pythia-6.9b} --- raw pretrained, 6.9B
        parameters, $\sim$300\,B training tokens, EleutherAI's
        open-source replication of GPT-NeoX.
  \item \texttt{lmsys/vicuna-7b-v1.3} --- LLaMA-1 7B base
        (1\,T pretraining tokens) with ShareGPT-style
        instruction-tuning supervised fine-tuning on top.
  \item \texttt{tiiuae/falcon-7b} --- raw pretrained, 7B parameters,
        $\sim$1.5\,T training tokens, TII's RefinedWeb-trained model
        with multi-query attention.

\subsection{Comparison metric}
\label{sec:tr-comparison-metric}

For each cell we compute the \emph{micro-averaged} instance-level
  agreement at \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9}: the fraction of
  \mbox{(\texttt{sample\_hash}, \texttt{evaluation\_result\_id})}
  rows whose per-metric \texttt{evaluation.score} is bit-identical
  between the public-HELM-side and local-side records.
Concretely:
\[
  \mathrm{agree\_ratio}_{\mathrm{cell}}
  \;=\;
  \frac{\sum_{(s, m) \in \mathrm{cell}}
       \mathbb{1}[\,
         |\mathrm{score}_{\mathrm{off}}(s, m)
          - \mathrm{score}_{\mathrm{loc}}(s, m)|
         < \epsilon
       \,]}
       {\sum_{(s, m) \in \mathrm{cell}} 1},
\]
with $\epsilon = 10^{-9}$.
The micro-average means a cell with multiple metric kinds is
  effectively weighted by per-metric row count, and any $0=0$
  collision between the two sides on a (sample, metric) row counts
  as a match --- a property that interacts with several of the
  failure modes we report and that we discuss in detail in
  Section~\ref{sec:tr-microavg}.

We deliberately use \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9} as the headline tolerance:
  for the binary-valued metrics that dominate the slim grid
  (\texttt{exact\_match}, the \texttt{*\_set\_match} family, etc.,
  whose per-row scores are $0$ or $1$), $10^{-9}$ is operationally
  indistinguishable from ``exact match'' --- the score gap between $0$
  and $1$ is $1.0$, far above any plausible floating-point drift ---
  and for continuous-valued metrics it pins any sub-$10^{-9}$
  numerical drift to ``agreement'' while still catching real
  per-instance flips.
Looser tolerances erase the per-instance flips this audit is
  designed to detect, and a relaxed tolerance hides the upstream
  effects we want to surface.
Per-cell tolerance sweeps are computed by the pipeline and are
  available in the cell-level JSON output, but the headline number is
  always at \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9}.

\section{Case Study A: \texorpdfstring{\texttt{entity\_matching}}{entity\_matching} pandas-merge ordering}
\label{sec:tr-case-a}
% =====================================================================

The three \texttt{entity\_matching} cells render as
  \texttt{join\_failed} for every model in the slim grid because the
  official-side and local-side \texttt{sample\_hash} sets have zero
  overlap, despite both sides drawing from the same Abt-Buy
  deepmatcher dataset and despite both sides exposing 1{,}000 instances
  with byte-equivalent \texttt{run\_spec.json} adapter specs.

\subsection{Symptom in the slim heatmap}
\label{sec:tr-case-a-symptom}

For each (model) on the slim grid, the per-side \eee{} record set
  exhibits the following pattern:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Both sides have $\sim 21{,}000$ sample records (1{,}000
        instances times $\sim 21$ metric records each).
  \item Both sides have $1{,}000$ unique \texttt{sample\_id} values
        (HELM's positional \texttt{id<i>} identifiers); the
        \texttt{sample\_id} overlap between sides is $1000/1000$.
  \item Each side has $\sim 996$--$999$ unique
        \texttt{sample\_hash} values (a few hashes appear on
        $> 1$ row each); the \texttt{sample\_hash} overlap between
        sides is $0/\sim999$ for every model.
  \item Both sides report $507$ \texttt{valid} + $493$ \texttt{test}
        instances, exactly matching across sides.
\end{itemize}

The two sides therefore agree on \emph{which positional indices into
  the eval pool got sampled}, but disagree on \emph{which dataset row
  each index points to}.
A concrete example for Pythia-6.9B: the official-side record with
  \texttt{sample\_id="id2221"} contains the prompt
  \texttt{Product A is name: panasonic silver dect 6.0 cordless telephone kxtg1033s.~description:~...},
  while the local-side record with the \emph{same}
  \texttt{sample\_id="id2221"} contains the prompt
  \texttt{Product A is name: samsung 19' black flat panel series 6 lcd hdtv ln19a650.~description:~...}.

\subsection{HELM downsample logic and instance-id assignment}
\label{sec:tr-case-a-helm-internals}

The relevant HELM source is concentrated in three files.
First, the downsampler at
  \texttt{helm/benchmark/runner.py:downsample\_eval\_instances}
  hardcodes the random seed:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python, caption={HELM v0.3.0 / v0.5.x \texttt{runner.py:downsample\_eval\_instances}.\label{lst:tr-helm-downsample}}]
def downsample_eval_instances(
    instances: List[Instance], max_eval_instances: int, eval_splits: List[str]
) -> List[Instance]:
    """
    Get the instances necessary for this run:
    Train instances (split=train): keep all (if any) for in-context learning
    Eval instances (split=valid or test): keep at most `max_eval_instances` specified in `AdapterSpec` by sampling
    Return the resulting train and eval instances.
    """
    all_train_instances: List[Instance] = [
        instance for instance in instances if instance.split == TRAIN_SPLIT
    ]
    all_eval_instances: List[Instance] = [
        instance for instance in instances if instance.split in eval_splits
    ]
    if len(all_eval_instances) > max_eval_instances:
        # The random sampling includes instances monotonically.
        np.random.seed(0)
        selected_eval_instances = list(
            np.random.choice(
                all_eval_instances,
                max_eval_instances,
                replace=False,
            )
        )
    else:
        selected_eval_instances = all_eval_instances
    return all_train_instances + selected_eval_instances
\end{lstlisting}

The seed is hardcoded \texttt{0}, so given the same input array length,
  \texttt{np.random.choice(arr, k, replace=False)} produces the same
  index sequence on every invocation.
Both sides have identical input array length and identical seeds; the
  sampled indices are therefore identical between sides.

Second, instance ids are assigned positionally over the
  scenario-emitted list \emph{before} the downsampler runs.
At \texttt{helm/benchmark/scenarios/scenario.py}:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python, caption={HELM \texttt{scenario.py:with\_instance\_ids}.\label{lst:tr-helm-with-instance-ids}}]
def with_instance_ids(instances: List[Instance]) -> List[Instance]:
    """Return the instances with an ID.  Note: order of instances matters."""
    return [replace(instance, id=f"id{i}") for i, instance in enumerate(instances)]
\end{lstlisting}

The docstring is explicit: \emph{order of instances matters}.
The instance id \texttt{id<i>} is by construction the positional
  index of the instance in the scenario-emitted list; any upstream
  reordering of that list silently rebinds id $\leftrightarrow$ content.

Third, the scenario itself
  (\texttt{helm/benchmark/scenarios/entity\_matching\_scenario.py})
  emits instances by reading three CSV splits and joining each with
  the lookup tables via \texttt{pd.merge}:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python, caption={HELM \texttt{entity\_matching\_scenario.py:read\_blocked\_pairs} (excerpt).\label{lst:tr-helm-read-blocked-pairs}}]
def read_blocked_pairs(self, data_path, split, tableA, tableB):
    """Read in pre-blocked pairs with T/F match labels."""
    labels = pd.read_csv(data_path / f"{split}.csv")
    mergedA = pd.merge(labels, tableA, right_on="id", left_on="ltable_id")
    merged = pd.merge(
        mergedA, tableB, right_on="id", left_on="rtable_id",
        suffixes=("_A", "_B"),
    )
    if split == TRAIN_SPLIT:
        num_pos_classes: int = sum(merged["label"] == 1)
        sample_fn = lambda x: x.sample(num_pos_classes)
        merged = merged.groupby("label", group_keys=False).apply(sample_fn)
    return merged
\end{lstlisting}

The two \texttt{pd.merge} calls produce a merged DataFrame whose row
  ordering depends on the pandas version's merge implementation.
The scenario then iterates the merged DataFrame in row order to emit
  Instance objects; \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} then assigns
  \texttt{id<i>} positionally; the downsampler then samples deterministic
  indices into the (possibly-reordered) list.

The mechanism is transparent in retrospect: same recipe, same data,
  same seed, same code, but different \texttt{pd.merge} row orderings
  between pandas versions $\Rightarrow$ different content at each
  positional index $\Rightarrow$ different
  $(\texttt{prompt}, \texttt{correct\_ref})$ pairs at each
  \texttt{id<i>} $\Rightarrow$ different
  \texttt{sample\_hash} values $\Rightarrow$ zero overlap.

\subsection{Pure-pandas minimal working example}
\label{sec:tr-case-a-pure-pandas-mwe}

We isolated the pandas-merge effect with a stand-alone reproducer that

  \texttt{read\_blocked\_pairs} on the byte-identical Abt-Buy

The reproducer scripts are at

\subsection{Three independent reproducibility leaks}
\label{sec:tr-case-a-three-leaks}

HELM PR \#1475 (commit \texttt{d69a6e6}, 2023-04-19, ``Fix random
  seeds for entity\_matching\_scenario'') added a fixed-numpy-random-state

The file at
  \texttt{helm/benchmark/scenarios/entity\_matching\_scenario\_fixed\_random\_state.py}

\begin{quote}\itshape
Set the fixed random state for entity\_matching\_scenario to ensure
  reproducibility.
Unfortunately, the previous official HELM runs did not initialize the
  numpy random state to zero, so future runs must use the same random
  states in order to sample the same test instances to reproduce the
  official HELM runs.
\end{quote}

The fix is necessary but not sufficient: it pins numpy's RNG state
  inside \texttt{get\_instances}, but the merge that determines which
  rows occupy each index sits upstream of the seed and is invisible
  to the fix.
Three independent reproducibility leaks survive the PR:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Pandas-merge ordering} (this case study).
        \texttt{pd.merge(\ldots, sort=False, how="inner")} does not
        formally guarantee output row order across pandas versions on
        all input shapes, and on the deepmatcher Abt-Buy data the
        order changed between pandas 2.0.x and 2.2.x.
  \item \textbf{Unchecksummed dataset download.}
        HELM's \texttt{ensure\_file\_downloaded} at
        \texttt{helm/common/general.py:80} treats the existence of the
        target path as proof of correctness:

        \begin{lstlisting}[style=python, caption={\texttt{helm/common/general.py:80} (excerpt).\label{lst:tr-helm-ensure-file-downloaded}}]
def ensure_file_downloaded(source_url, target_path, unpack=False, ...):
    """Download `source_url` to `target_path` if it doesn't exist."""
    with FileLock(f"{target_path}.lock"):
        if os.path.exists(target_path):
            # Assume it's all good
            hlog(f"Not downloading {source_url} because {target_path} already exists")
            return
        ...
        \end{lstlisting}

        A stale or corrupted local cache silently shadows the remote
        dataset.
        For the slim-paper run, the upstream Wisconsin CS host has
        not modified the deepmatcher data since 2018, so this leak
        does not manifest in our case --- but it is a latent
        reproducibility hazard.
  \item \textbf{Positional id binding.}
        \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} at
        \texttt{helm/benchmark/scenarios/scenario.py:284} binds
        \texttt{id<i>} to the scenario-emit position, so any upstream
        ordering instability silently rebinds id $\leftrightarrow$
        content without surfacing in the run-spec hash.
        This leak is what amplifies the pandas-merge effect into a
        same-id-different-content pattern that
        downstream consumers cannot detect via id-based comparison.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What \eee{} catches that HELM's id-based comparison misses}
\label{sec:tr-case-a-eee-catches}

A consumer who joins the two sides on
  \mbox{(\texttt{sample\_id}, \texttt{evaluation\_result\_id})} would
  observe perfect $1000/1000$ overlap and infer that the runs agree
  on which examples were evaluated.
That inference is wrong: the prompts under each id are completely
  different.
Joining on the content-derived
  \mbox{(\texttt{sample\_hash}, \texttt{evaluation\_result\_id})}
  correctly rejects the join, because the prompts, although under the
  same \texttt{id<i>}, refer to different concrete product pairs and
  therefore hash to different values.

The \texttt{join\_failed} cell on the slim heatmap is therefore not a
  flaw in the audit: it is the audit correctly refusing to assert
  agreement on samples whose content disagrees.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Why the $0.788$ cell value understates the gap}{Why the 0.788 cell value understates the gap}}
\label{sec:tr-case-b-understates}

The headline cell-level agreement of $0.788$ is a micro-average over
  $3000$ \mbox{(sample, metric)} rows; per-metric it breaks down as
  $\{0.819, 0.772, 0.772\}$ for
  \texttt{exact\_set\_match}, \texttt{f1\_set\_match}, and
  \texttt{iou\_set\_match} respectively.
Three independent reasons combine to make $0.788$ understate the
  underlying disagreement; we discuss each in turn here and revisit
  them as a class of micro-average failure modes in
  Section~\ref{sec:tr-microavg}.

First, \emph{per-metric averaging} is the smallest effect: the three
  per-metric values cluster within $0.05$ of each other, so the
  micro-average is close to any individual per-metric value.
This is not the dominant problem.

Second, \emph{degenerate-zero agreement} is the dominant effect.
With the official run scoring $0.0$ on every row, every $\text{LOCAL}=0$
  outcome counts as a $0=0$ agreement at \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9}.
The $0.788$ thus reads as ``fraction of prompts on which the local
  side also scored zero'', not ``fraction of cases where the two model
  behaviours agree''.
The local model also scores $0$ on $\sim 78\%$ of prompts (the model
  is genuinely bad at the task at this scale), so $\sim 78\%$ of
  rows are $0=0$ collisions.
The remaining $\sim 22\%$ of rows are cases where the local model
  scored above $0$ on a prompt that the official scored at $0$ ---
  i.e., the local side did better than the official side on those
  prompts.
The $0.788$ is therefore not a ``$78.8\%$ reproducibility'' reading;
  it is a ``$78.8\%$ both-failed-together'' reading.

Third, \emph{aggregate-score divergence}: the run-level means from
  \texttt{stats.json} are $0.000$ for the three set-match metrics
  on the official side and $0.215$, $0.208$, $0.185$ on the local
  side.
At the leaderboard-cited number level, the two runs differ by the
  entire model accuracy ($\sim 20$ percentage points absolute).
The instance-level $0.788$ does not capture this gap.

\section{Case Study C: \texorpdfstring{\texttt{wikifact} ($\approx 0.92$ across all three models)}{wikifact (approx 0.92 across all three models)} --- stochastic noise floor}
\label{sec:tr-case-c}
% =====================================================================

The three \texttt{wikifact} cells agree at $0.920$, $0.922$, $0.927$
  for Vicuna, Pythia, and Falcon respectively.
The recipe (verified bytewise on both sides) is
  \texttt{temperature=1.0, num\_outputs=5, max\_tokens=8,
  stop\_sequences=[`\textbackslash n']}.
HELM uses \texttt{temperature=1.0} on \texttt{wikifact} to draw five
  diverse outputs for the \texttt{exact\_match@5} family of metrics.
Two independent runs of a stochastic recipe are not bitwise
  reproducible by construction, and we predict the observed $\approx
  0.92$ from a Bernoulli model whose key identity is mechanically
  proved in our companion Lean file
  \texttt{wikifact\_consistency\_claim.lean}.

\subsection{The Bernoulli model and its variance decomposition}
\label{sec:tr-case-c-math}

For a binary metric with per-prompt hit rate $p_i$ (defined as the
  per-prompt probability that the metric scores $1$ on a single
  stochastic run, i.e., the per-prompt \texttt{exact\_match@5}
  success probability), the expected agreement of two independent
  stochastic runs over a benchmark of $N$ prompts is
\[
  \mathbb{E}_i[\,\text{agree}(p_i)\,]
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}_i[\,p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2\,].
\]

The Lean-verified central identity (theorem
  \texttt{expectedAgreement\_eq\_uniformAgreement\_add\_variance}
  in \texttt{wikifact\_consistency\_claim.lean}) decomposes this as
\[
  \mathbb{E}_i[\,\text{agree}(p_i)\,]
  \;=\;
  \text{agree}(\bar p)
  \;+\; 2\,\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i),
\]
where $\bar p = \mathbb{E}_i[p_i]$ is the prompt-averaged hit rate
  and $\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i) = \mathbb{E}_i[(p_i - \bar p)^2]$.
The first term is the homogeneous-Bernoulli prediction one would
  make if every prompt had the same hit rate $\bar p$; the second is
  a non-negative variance term that captures prompt-level
  heterogeneity.
Together they imply (theorem
  \texttt{uniformAgreement\_le\_expectedAgreement}) that
  $\text{agree}(\bar p) \le \mathbb{E}_i[\text{agree}(p_i)]$: the
  homogeneous prediction at the mean is a lower bound on the
  heterogeneous expected agreement.

\subsection{Numerical values for the homogeneous prediction}
\label{sec:tr-case-c-numerics}

The homogeneous prediction is
$\text{agree}(p) = 2(p - \tfrac{1}{2})^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}$
  (Lean theorem \texttt{agree\_centered}).

The implication is sharp.
The homogeneous Bernoulli equation $p^2 + (1-p)^2 = 0.92$ has roots
\[
  p = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(1 \pm \sqrt{0.84}\bigr)
    \approx 0.9583 \;\text{or}\; 0.0417,
\]
so any $\bar p \in (0.0417, 0.9583)$ falls strictly below the
  observed $0.92$ in the homogeneous case.
Producing a heterogeneous expected agreement of exactly $\approx 0.92$
  therefore requires either $\bar p \approx 0.958$ (or its symmetric
  twin $\approx 0.042$, equally well-supported by the formula but
  unrealistic for trained models on this benchmark) or non-trivial
  $\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i)$.
The latter --- prompt-level heterogeneity, with many prompts being
  near-deterministic --- is what one would expect for a trained model
  on a benchmark with prompt-dependent difficulty
  (some prompts the model gets consistently right, others
  consistently wrong).

\subsection{Cross-model uniformity diagnostic}
\label{sec:tr-case-c-uniformity}

Our observed $\approx 0.92$ across all three models is consistent
  with the latter regime, and the cross-model uniformity
  (Pythia at $0.922$ vs.\ Vicuna at $0.920$ and Falcon at $0.927$)
  is itself diagnostic: Pythia is a systematic outlier on every other
  benchmark in the table (Section~\ref{sec:tr-pythia-pattern}), but
  on \texttt{wikifact} it converges to the same $\approx 0.92$ the
  instruction-tuned and Falcon variants achieve.
This supports the interpretation that recipe-induced stochasticity
  is the main driver of disagreement on this cell rather than any
  model-specific backend effect.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{The 1-vs-5 EEE \texttt{output.raw} converter cosmetic}{The 1-vs-5 EEE output.raw converter cosmetic}}

\paragraph{Pattern.}
On multiple-choice cells (LSAT-QA, MMLU, TruthfulQA), the
  disagreement manifests as a clean letter flip at the model's
  greedy first generated token: the model emits one of A--E, and the
  two backends disagree on which letter wins on a small fraction of
  prompts (typically $4$--$6\%$).
On short-answer cells (BoolQ, CivilComments), the disagreement is a
  label flip (\texttt{Yes}/\texttt{No} or
  \texttt{True}/\texttt{False}) at the same greedy first-token
  position; same shape, different vocabulary, same $\sim 5\%$ rate.

On free-form cells (QuAC, NarrativeQA), the divergence point is not
  necessarily the first generated token.
For example, in the QuAC sample shown the prompt-determined
  prefix does not exist (the model is starting fresh after
  ``Answer:''), but the model can either commit to an
  off-topic generation or to the \texttt{CANNOTANSWER} stem; once
  one or the other is chosen, the rest of the generation diverges.
The mechanism is the same first-divergent-token argmax flip; what
  differs is how late in the generation the divergence happens.
For chain-of-thought cells (GSM, not at 0.98 boundary; see
  Section~\ref{sec:tr-spot-check-near-perfect}), the divergence can
  be 50 tokens or more into the response.

\subsection{Spot-check: high-but-not-perfect cells (0.99x)}
\label{sec:tr-spot-check-near-perfect}

To verify that the Pythia outlier mechanism is the same as the
  near-perfect cells, we spot-checked four high-agreement cells:
  SR~$\times$~Falcon-7B (0.999), SR~$\times$~Vicuna-7B (0.997),
  GSM~$\times$~Vicuna (0.990), and GSM~$\times$~Falcon (0.996).

\paragraph{SR~$\times$~Falcon-7B (0.999).}
Recipe: \texttt{temp=0.0, num\_outputs=1, max\_tokens=50}.
Per-metric breakdown:
  \texttt{exact\_match} agrees $1000/1000$;
  \texttt{prefix\_exact\_match} agrees $999/1000$;
  \texttt{quasi\_exact\_match} agrees $998/1000$;
  \texttt{quasi\_prefix\_exact\_match} agrees $1000/1000$.
The cell-level micro-average is $3997/4000 = 0.9992$.

A direct count of bytewise-different \texttt{output.raw} between the
  two sides reveals 10 of 1000 prompts ($1.0\%$) with divergent
  outputs; only 3 of those 10 produce score disagreements across the
  four core metrics, because on the other 7 divergent prompts both
  outputs miss the gold reference and both score $0$.
The cell value of $0.999$ thus hides a $\sim 10\times$ larger
  output-divergence rate; we discuss this case as the canonical
  example of \emph{both-wrong masking} in
  Section~\ref{sec:tr-microavg}.

\paragraph{SR~$\times$~Vicuna-7B (0.997).}
Recipe: \texttt{temp=0.0, num\_outputs=1, max\_tokens=20}.
$2/1000$ \texttt{exact\_match} rows disagree; both examples show
  divergent first tokens (e.g., target
  \texttt{`* = whale whale -'} --- official emits
  \texttt{`* whale whale whale - = -'} (wrong), local emits
  \texttt{`* = whale whale -'} (correct)).

\paragraph{GSM~$\times$~Vicuna-7B (0.990).}
Recipe: \texttt{temp=0.0, num\_outputs=1, max\_tokens=400,
  stop\_sequences=['\textbackslash n\textbackslash n']} --- long
  chain-of-thought.
$10/1000$ \texttt{exact\_match\_indicator} rows disagree.
Concrete example: a prompt about ``carrots \& sweet potatoes''
  produces a chain whose first $\sim 70$ characters are byte-identical
  on both sides
  (``The total weight of the sweet potatoes is 2 \textbackslash * 5 =
  $\langle\langle$2*5=10$\rangle\rangle$10 pounds The total weight of
  the \dots''),
  then official continues
  ``\textbf{carro}[ts]'' (right path) and local continues
  ``\textbf{potat}[oes]'' (wrong path).
The divergence at character $\sim 70$ amplifies through the rest of
  the generation; the F1 / \texttt{exact\_match\_indicator} metric
  picks up the resulting score flip.

\paragraph{GSM~$\times$~Falcon-7B (0.996).}
Same recipe; $4/1000$ rows disagree; similar mid-chain divergence.

The pattern is the same first-divergent-token-argmax-flip mechanism
  across all four near-perfect cells, just at much smaller magnitude
  than the Pythia outliers.

\subsection{Logit-margin hypothesis}
\label{sec:tr-pythia-logit-margin}

We hypothesize that Pythia-6.9B's higher inference-stack sensitivity
  reflects \emph{narrower per-token logit margins}.
The mechanism: when one inference stack serves at \texttt{fp16} or
  \texttt{bf16} and another at \texttt{fp32}, the resulting logit
  perturbations between the two stacks are typically on the order of
  $10^{-4}$ in the body of the next-token distribution.
That perturbation flips the greedy argmax only when the top-1 and
  top-2 logits at the relevant decoding position are within
  $\sim 10^{-4}$ of each other.
How often that happens depends on \emph{how peaked} the model's
  next-token distribution is at the position in question, which in
  turn depends on training compute and post-pretraining alignment.
Table~\ref{tab:tr-pythia-factors} summarises the relevant axes for
  the three slim-heatmap models.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small
\caption{Factors plausibly relevant to inference-stack sensitivity
  for the three slim-heatmap models.
The two axes most consistent with the observed sensitivity ranking
  (Pythia $\gg$ Vicuna $>$ Falcon) are training compute and presence
  of instruction tuning; both reduce the prevalence of close-margin
  greedy decisions.}
\label{tab:tr-pythia-factors}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
factor & Pythia-6.9B & Vicuna-7B-v1.3 & Falcon-7B \\
\midrule
training compute (tokens) & $\sim$300\,B & 1\,T (LLaMA-1 base) + SFT & 1.5\,T \\

\bottomrule

The two axes consistent with our sensitivity ranking are training
  compute and instruction tuning.
Tokenizer family does \emph{not} explain the pattern (Pythia and
  Falcon share the GPT-NeoX BPE tokenizer but show very different
  sensitivities), and attention architecture is too entangled with
  training-time effects to isolate from this evidence.

\subsection{What we cannot rule out from three-model evidence}
\label{sec:tr-pythia-confounds}

The logit-margin hypothesis above is consistent with our observations
  but not proven by them.
Confounds we cannot rule out without further experiments:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Different served checkpoint.}
        Together may host a Pythia-6.9B revision or quantization that
        differs from the HuggingFace Hub checkpoint our local audit
        uses, in which case the divergence is partly a ``wrong
        weights'' effect rather than a numerical-precision effect.
        We did not directly compare the served weights.
  \item \textbf{Different serving precision.}
        We do not know the exact serving precision Together used for
        Pythia-6.9B in the historical HELM Classic v0.3.0 runs.
        Different precisions (\texttt{fp16}, \texttt{bf16},
        \texttt{int8}) produce different perturbation magnitudes;
        \texttt{int8} in particular would explain a much higher
        sensitivity than \texttt{fp16}.
  \item \textbf{Architecture-specific numerical sensitivity.}
        Falcon's multi-query attention reduces the number of KV-heads
        and is empirically more numerically stable under
        \texttt{fp16} serving than standard multi-head attention.
        Some of Falcon's apparent insensitivity may be the
        architecture rather than the training compute or alignment.
  \item \textbf{Generation-kernel differences.}
        Together's hosted-inference stack and HuggingFace's
        \texttt{transformers} use different fused-kernel paths for
        attention and softmax; differences in those paths can produce
        small per-position logit drift independently of the model's
        precision.
\end{itemize}

Distinguishing among these would require A/B testing against
  Together's current API at known precision, which we have not done.
Such an experiment would not change the case-study claim --- that the
  divergence exists, is detected by EEE's \texttt{sample\_hash}, is
  consistent with a backend-substitution effect, and disproportionately
  affects the model with the smallest training-compute budget and no
  instruction tuning --- but would localize the proximate cause to one
  of the four candidates above.

\section{Methodological Caveats: Micro-Averaged Agreement}
\label{sec:tr-microavg}
% =====================================================================

The headline cell-level agree\_ratio is a micro-average over
  \mbox{(sample, metric)} rows: the cell value is
  $\sum \text{matched} / \sum \text{count}$ across all rows, weighting
  each row equally.
This is the right summary statistic for a reproducibility heatmap
  whose readers want to know ``what fraction of per-instance per-metric
  scores agree?''
But three failure modes of this micro-average are visible in the slim
  heatmap and worth surfacing for any reader of a reproducibility
  report; all three flow from the same property: any $0=0$ (or
  identical-score) collision between the two sides counts as a match,
  even when the underlying outputs differ.

\subsection{Failure mode I: degenerate-zero agreement (catastrophic)}
\label{sec:tr-microavg-degenerate}

When one side's run is degenerate
  (e.g., the SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia broken official run scoring
  $0$ on every row), every \mbox{(sample, metric)} row where the
  other side also scores $0$ counts as agreement at \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9}.
The agreement rate then reads as ``fraction of prompts on which the
  non-degenerate side also failed'', not ``fraction of cases where
  the two model behaviours agree''.
SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia at $0.788$ is this case: every
  ``agreement'' is a $0=0$ collision on a hard prompt, while the
  underlying run-level means are $0.000$ official and $\approx 0.20$
  local.

\subsection{Failure mode II: both-wrong masking (mild)}
\label{sec:tr-microavg-both-wrong}

The same mechanism appears in milder form on cells where the model
  is moderately bad at the benchmark and the metric scores $0$ on
  most prompts.
On SR~$\times$~Falcon-7B (cell agree\_ratio $0.999$), $10$ of
  $1000$ prompts have bytewise-different \texttt{output.raw} between
  Together and HF --- a $1.0\%$ true model-output divergence rate.
But of those $10$ output-divergent prompts, only $3$ produce score
  disagreements across the four core metrics
  (\texttt{exact\_match}, \texttt{prefix\_exact\_match},
  \texttt{quasi\_exact\_match}, \texttt{quasi\_prefix\_exact\_match})
  $\times$ $1000$ prompts $=$ $4000$ score rows, because the other
  $7$ output-divergent prompts produce outputs that both miss the
  gold reference, so the score is $0=0$ on every metric and the row
  counts as ``agreement''.
The cell-level number $0.9992$ thus hides a $\sim 10\times$ larger
  output-divergence rate.
This is not a flaw in the metric --- it accurately reports score
  agreement --- but a reader who interprets the cell as ``the models
  emit nearly identical outputs'' is wrong by an order of magnitude.

\subsection{Failure mode III: stochastic noise floor}
\label{sec:tr-microavg-stochastic}

For recipes with \texttt{temperature>0}, two independent runs are not
  bitwise reproducible by design.
The expected agreement under a per-prompt Bernoulli model with
  per-prompt hit rate $p_i$ (the per-prompt
  \texttt{exact\_match@k} probability) decomposes exactly as
  $\mathbb{E}_i[\,\text{agree}(p_i)\,] = \text{agree}(\bar p) + 2\,
  \mathrm{Var}_i(p_i)$
  (Section~\ref{sec:tr-case-c}).
\texttt{wikifact} at $\approx 0.92$ across all three models is
  consistent with prompt-level heterogeneity in $p_i$ --- many prompts
  near-deterministic, which is what one would expect for a trained
  model on a benchmark with prompt-dependent difficulty.
A reviewer who interprets the cell as ``92\% reproducibility'' rather
  than ``stochastic recipe at the heterogeneity-corrected expected
  agreement'' is missing the recipe-level non-determinism the
  benchmark explicitly enables.

\subsection{What honest reporting looks like}
\label{sec:tr-microavg-honest}

A reproducibility report that surfaces \texttt{agree\_ratio} alone
  cannot distinguish the three cases above from a recipe-clean
  near-perfect reproduction at $0.92$ or $0.999$.
Honest reporting wants to surface, alongside \texttt{agree\_ratio},
  at least three additional signals:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Run-level mean per metric on each side, to expose the
        degenerate-zero case.
  \item The recipe's \texttt{temperature} setting, to expose the
        stochastic-floor case.
  \item The output-divergence rate
        $\Pr_i[\,\texttt{output.raw}_{\mathrm{off}}^{(i)} \neq
        \texttt{output.raw}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(i)}\,]$,
        to expose the both-wrong-masking case.
\end{enumerate}

The pipeline released alongside this report emits the first two
  alongside the cell-level number; a future iteration of the heatmap
  visualization could surface all three inline, e.g., as a per-cell
  tuple of (micro-averaged agree\_ratio, official mean, local mean,
  output-divergence rate), so a reader can flag cells where the
  agreement reading is misleading without leaving the figure.

\section{EEE Audit/Forensics Scope}
\label{sec:tr-scope-split}
% =====================================================================

\eee{} is sufficient for audit (detecting whether two runs agree, by
  how much, on which instances, and at what cell-level granularity)
  but not sufficient for forensics (identifying \emph{why} the
  disagreement occurred).
Each of the three case studies in this report made the
  audit/forensics split visible in concrete terms:
  Case~A's pandas-merge mechanism required inspecting HELM's
  \texttt{run\_spec.json} adapter spec, the
  \texttt{scenario\_state.json} per-instance ordering, and the
  \texttt{entity\_matching\_scenario.py} source;
  Case~B's Together-hosted-Pythia mechanism required HELM's
  \texttt{auto\_client.py} dispatch logic;
  Case~C's noise-floor diagnosis required the
  \texttt{run\_spec.json} \texttt{temperature} field.

\eee{} preserved the divergence signal on all three; it did not
  preserve the upstream framework version, the inference backend
  identity, or the precision/kernel of the generation step.

\subsection{Per-question scope table}
\label{sec:tr-scope-table}

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small
\caption{Per-question audit/forensics scope split for \eee{}.
The first half of the table (audit-side questions) is what \eee{}
  is designed to answer; the second half (forensics-side questions)
  is what typically requires upstream framework artifacts.}
\label{tab:tr-scope-split}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
question & answerable from EEE alone? \\
\midrule
Did these two runs agree? & yes \\
By how much do they disagree at instance level? & yes \\
At which instances? & yes (\texttt{sample\_hash} join $\to$ per-instance abs\_delta) \\
What did the model see? & yes (\texttt{input.raw}) \\
What did the model output? & yes (\texttt{output.raw}) \\
What did the evaluator extract? & yes (\texttt{answer\_attribution.extracted\_value}) \\
What was the run-level mean per metric? & yes (\texttt{evaluation.score} $\to$ run-level mean) \\
\midrule
What was the inference backend (Together / vLLM / HF / \dots)? & no \\
What was the runtime resolution of \texttt{model\_deployment} when null? & no \\
What inference precision (fp32 / fp16 / bf16) and attention kernel? & no \\
What \texttt{transformers} / \texttt{pandas} / \texttt{numpy} / framework versions? & partial: \texttt{eval\_library\_version} recorded but typically \texttt{"unknown"} \\
Why do two runs of the same recipe diverge? & no --- typically requires upstream framework artifacts + source \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Why the split is a feature, not a flaw}
\label{sec:tr-scope-feature}

The audit layer is designed to be small, framework-neutral, and
  stable across harness versions; the forensics layer is necessarily
  framework-specific (different harnesses pin recipe in different ways)
  and benefits from being kept separate so the audit format does not
  have to track every harness's evolution.
A version of \eee{} that preserved the framework run-time artifacts
  would replicate the very provenance fragility the case studies
  surface: the \texttt{run\_spec.json} schema evolves across HELM
  releases, the \texttt{auto\_client.py} dispatch table changes, and
  the field that determines deployment routing today may not exist a
  year from now.
The audit/forensics split protects \eee{} consumers from that
  evolution: an EEE artifact computed today remains parsable and
  auditable by a consumer five years from now even if HELM v1.x has
  reorganized its source tree.

\section{Tooling Deliverables}
\label{sec:tr-tooling}

The audit produced four reproducer tools and four code changes to the
  audit pipeline, consistent with the executive-summary count
  (Section~\ref{sec:tr-summary}).
All deliverables are released alongside this report under
  \texttt{dev/oneoff/} and \texttt{eval\_audit/} in the repository.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\texttt{audit\_eee\_only\_run.py}}{audit\_eee\_only\_run.py}}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-audit-hook}

The runtime EEE-only paper-validity hook described in
  Section~\ref{sec:tr-paper-validity-guard}.
Wraps a command (typically \texttt{20\_run.sh}) under a
  \texttt{sitecustomize.py}-injected \texttt{sys.addaudithook} that
  records every \texttt{open} / \texttt{os.open} event from every
  child Python process to per-PID JSONL logs, classifies each
  recorded path against a HELM-shape signature, and emits a
  PASS/FAIL verdict.

Verdict for the slim-paper run: PASS, $0$ flagged opens of
  $205{,}798$ total events across $88$ child Python processes.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\texttt{em\_pandas\_mwe.sh} + \texttt{em\_pandas\_mwe\_run.py}}{em\_pandas\_mwe.sh + em\_pandas\_mwe\_run.py}}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-em-pandas-mwe}

The pure-pandas isolation reproducer for Case~A.
Builds five venvs at distinct \mbox{(pandas, numpy)} version pairs,
  runs only the merge sequence from
  \texttt{EntityMatchingScenario.read\_blocked\_pairs} against the
  byte-identical Abt-Buy deepmatcher CSVs, and reports a digest
  comparison.
The grid is laid out so the pandas-version axis can be isolated from
  the numpy-version axis; on the slim-paper run, the digest collapses
  to two distinct values across the five combos and the boundary is
  purely pandas $2.0 \to 2.2$
  (Section~\ref{sec:tr-case-a-pure-pandas-mwe},
  Table~\ref{tab:tr-em-pandas-mwe}).

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\texttt{em\_helm\_mwe.sh} + \texttt{em\_helm\_mwe\_run.py}}{em\_helm\_mwe.sh + em\_helm\_mwe\_run.py}}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-em-helm-mwe}

The end-to-end HELM-in-the-loop reproducer for Case~A.
Builds a current venv and a v0.3.0-era venv
  (\texttt{crfm-helm==0.3.0 + pandas==2.0.3 + numpy==1.23.5},
  Python 3.10), runs the full HELM scenario chain
  (\texttt{EntityMatchingScenario.get\_instances} $\to$
  \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} $\to$
  \texttt{downsample\_eval\_instances}) inside each, and diffs the
  resulting ordered \mbox{(rank, id, content\_sig)} sequence against
  captured \texttt{scenario\_state.json} files from real HELM runs.
Emits a 4-cell verdict matrix
  (Section~\ref{sec:tr-case-a-helm-mwe},

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\texttt{diagnose\_entity\_matching\_join.py}}{diagnose\_entity\_matching\_join.py}}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-em-diagnose}

The Q1--Q5 diagnostic for the entity\_matching join failure
  (read-only).
Five questions: content-overlap (relabel vs.\ disjoint?), id
  permutation (when shared content exists, what's the off~$\to$~loc
  map?), \texttt{sample\_hash} function inspection, fewshot vs.\
  test-pair stability, and HELM-shaped metadata sniff.
Plus a cross-model diagnostic: whether \texttt{id<i>} on the
  official side is internally consistent across all three model
  conversions.
The output of this diagnostic is what initially identified the
  pandas-merge mechanism.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Metric-classifier patch + \texttt{no\_core\_metrics} status}{Metric-classifier patch + no\_core\_metrics status}}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-classifier}

\paragraph{Set-match metric registration.}
The metric classifier at \texttt{eval\_audit/helm/metrics.py} did
  not list the \texttt{*\_set\_match} family in
  \texttt{CORE\_PREFIXES}, so \texttt{f1\_set\_match},
  \texttt{iou\_set\_match}, and \texttt{exact\_set\_match} (the
  metrics used by \texttt{synthetic\_reasoning\_natural}) were
  classified as \texttt{untracked} and the comparator's class
  filter dropped 100\% of the rows.
The result was a \texttt{join\_failed} render despite the
  \texttt{sample\_hash} overlap being $1000/1000$.
The fix (commit \texttt{36fc52e}) added the three prefixes to
  \texttt{CORE\_PREFIXES}.

\paragraph{New \texttt{no\_core\_metrics} status.}
The same commit added a fourth heatmap status (after
  \texttt{present}, \texttt{join\_failed}, \texttt{missing}) that
  distinguishes ``data joined but classifier filter dropped all
  rows'' from ``data didn't join''.
The status is only emitted when \texttt{n\_joined\_pairs > 0} but
  \texttt{count == 0}.
On the slim-paper run, the metric-classifier fix landed first and
  the \texttt{synthetic\_reasoning\_natural} cells turned from
  \texttt{join\_failed} to \texttt{present}; no cell on the slim grid
  currently renders as \texttt{no\_core\_metrics}.
The status remains in the heatmap collector for future runs where the
  classifier may be missing a metric family.

The new status required threading a pre-classifier-filter join count
  (\texttt{n\_joined\_pairs}) through the pipeline:
  \texttt{eval\_audit/normalized/compare.py} emits it,
  \texttt{eval\_audit/reports/core\_metrics.py} writes it into
  \texttt{core\_metric\_report.json}, and
  \texttt{eval\_audit/reports/eee\_only\_heatmap.py} reads it from
  the per-pair report and assigns the new status when appropriate.
A subsequent commit (\texttt{77d90af}) propagated the field into
  \texttt{cell\_data.json} so downstream consumers can read it
  without re-walking the per-pair reports.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\texttt{redraw\_plots.sh}}{redraw\_plots.sh} auto-generation}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-redraw-plots}

The heatmap renderer
  (\texttt{eval\_audit/reports/eee\_only\_heatmap.py}) now drops a
  self-contained \texttt{redraw\_plots.sh} into the heatmap output
  directory at every render.
The script captures the exact invocation used to produce the
  outputs (\texttt{--analysis-root}, \texttt{--abs-tol},
  \texttt{--title}, \texttt{--per-metric} flag) and forwards
  \texttt{"\$@"} so the user can override at re-run time.
Output dir is resolved as the script's own \texttt{\$SCRIPT\_DIR},
  so the script remains valid if the heatmap dir is copied or moved.
The repo-root resolution uses the same
  \texttt{portable\_repo\_root\_lines} helper as the per-packet
  reproduce scripts emitted by \texttt{rebuild\_core\_report.py}.

The script also captures the colour-scale env vars
  (\texttt{EVAL\_AUDIT\_HEATMAP\_VMIN} /
  \texttt{EVAL\_AUDIT\_HEATMAP\_VMAX}) at generation time via the
  \texttt{\${VAR:-default}} indirection, so re-runs match the
  original colour scale unless the user explicitly overrides via the
  same env vars.

\subsection{Heatmap colour-scale + dynamic legend}
\label{sec:tr-tooling-heatmap-ux}

For paper presentation we tightened the heatmap colour scale and
  cleaned up the legend.
The colour scale defaults to \texttt{vmin=0.7, vmax=1.0} (configurable
  via env vars), so the actual data range $[0.788, 1.000]$ spans the
  full gradient instead of clustering in the green band.
The legend is built dynamically from the cell statuses actually
  present in the heatmap: the slim-paper render shows just
  \texttt{present} + \texttt{join\_failed}; \texttt{no\_core\_metrics}
  and \texttt{missing} are correctly omitted because no cell on this
  grid has those statuses.
The \texttt{present} legend swatch uses \texttt{cmap(vmax)} (deep
  green) so it matches what readers see on the actual high-agreement
  cells.

\section{Limitations and Follow-Ups}

\subsection{Limitations of this audit}
\label{sec:tr-limitations-list}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Closed-API and hosted-only models.}
        The audit covers only open-weight models for which a local
        re-execution is meaningful; we did not attempt to audit
        Together-hosted-only or API-only models.
        Auditing them would require endpoint snapshotting, which is
        its own provenance-preservation problem.
  \item \textbf{Multi-turn and agentic evaluations.}
        HELM's multi-turn and agent-tool scenario classes introduce
        additional provenance layers (agent scaffold version, tool
        registry, sandbox snapshot) not exercised here.
        The slim grid is single-turn only.
  \item \textbf{Cross-harness alignment.}
        The audit does not align HELM scores against
        \texttt{lm-eval-harness} or \texttt{Inspect-AI} scores for
        the same model and benchmark, which would introduce a second
        axis of harness-level provenance drift.
  \item \textbf{Together-side direct verification of Case~B.}
        We have direct evidence that the public-side Pythia run was
        Together-routed (HELM v0.3.0 \texttt{auto\_client.py}
        dispatch logic) and that its outputs match the
        empty-completion pattern, but we did not call Together's API
        directly to verify that today's Together-hosted Pythia
        reproduces the 2023-era empty-completion behaviour.
        Such a verification would be a useful follow-on if a reviewer
        pushes back on the Case~B mechanism, but is not strictly
        required for the audit-level claim that the public HELM
        Classic v0.3.0 SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia score is an
        artifact rather than a capability measurement.
  \item \textbf{Quantification of the systematic Pythia pattern.}
        Section~\ref{sec:tr-pythia-pattern} characterizes the eight
        Pythia outlier cells qualitatively, but does not produce a
        per-cell quantification of how much of each agreement gap is
        attributable to backend substitution versus any other
        effect.
        A more thorough version would re-run each Pythia cell on a
        HuggingFace-deployment-only public-side mirror (if one were
        available) and quantify the residual.
  \item \textbf{Cross-machine validation.}
        The local audit was run on a single host (\texttt{aiq-gpu});
        cross-machine validation (running the same scope on a
        different host with different hardware) was not exercised
        for this report.
  \item \textbf{Single time-point.}
        The Case~B Together-routing finding is anchored in HELM v0.3.0
        (Nov 2023) and HELM v0.5.x (current); we did not test
        intermediate HELM versions or verify against historical
        Together API behaviour.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Follow-up experiments}
\label{sec:tr-followups}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Direct Together API call.}
        Inference Pythia-6.9B against today's Together API at known
        precision and compare the first-token distribution to our
        local HuggingFace serving.
        Would localize the proximate cause of the Pythia divergence
        to one of the four candidates listed in
        Section~\ref{sec:tr-pythia-confounds}.
  \item \textbf{Bisect the exact pandas commit.}
        Narrow the Case~A pandas-merge boundary from
        $2.0 \to 2.2$ to a specific point release and ideally the
        upstream commit that introduced the merge-ordering change.
        Would let us file a precise upstream bug or document a
        specific compatibility note.
  \item \textbf{Cross-harness audit.}
        Run the same three-model slim grid through
        \texttt{lm-eval-harness} or \texttt{Inspect-AI} and align
        per-instance scores against the HELM-side records via
        \eee{}.
        Would surface a second axis of harness-level drift and
        validate \eee{}'s cross-harness comparability claim from a
        different angle.
  \item \textbf{Heatmap UX with output-divergence rate inline.}
        The methodological caveats in
        Section~\ref{sec:tr-microavg} would benefit from being
        surfaced inline in the heatmap, e.g.\ as a per-cell tuple of
        (micro-averaged agree\_ratio, official mean, local mean,
        output-divergence rate).
        A future iteration of the heatmap renderer could compute
        and display all four.
\end{itemize}
