\section{HELM Instance-Level Reproducibility Audit (Case Study 3)}
\label{sec:app-helm-audit-v2}

This appendix is the long-form writeup of the HELM-side reproducibility
  audit summarised in Section~\ref{sec:cs3-helm}.
The audit operates on the slim slice
  (Pythia~6.9B, Vicuna~7B v1.3, Falcon~7B; fourteen Classic v0.3.0
  benchmarks; 42 \mbox{(model, benchmark)} cells) and focuses on three
  named reproducibility-failure mechanisms that surface from the
  instance-level agreement distribution.
We document the audit pipeline, the runtime EEE-only validation that
  guarantees the published numbers were produced from EEE artifacts
  alone, the three case studies in detail, the systematic
  Pythia-Together-vs-HuggingFace pattern that the case studies
  generalize into, and two methodological caveats about how
  micro-averaged instance-level agreement reads when one side is
  degenerate or the recipe is stochastic.
The artifacts in this appendix were regenerated end-to-end by the
  pipeline released alongside this paper from the public HELM corpus
  and the local audit runs; the runbook is three thin shell scripts,
  \texttt{10\_link\_tree.sh} / \texttt{20\_run.sh} / \texttt{30\_heatmap.sh}
  under
  \path{reproduce/eee_only_reproducibility_heatmap/}.

\subsection{Motivating Question}
\label{sec:app-helm-question-v2}

The case study answers the operational question:
\begin{quote}\itshape
For a published HELM evaluation result, can a downstream consumer who
  has only the published artifacts (a) detect whether a re-execution
  agrees with the published number, (b) localize where any
  disagreement comes from, and (c) reach a defensible verdict about
  whether the disagreement reflects a real reproducibility failure or
  an expected artifact of the evaluation recipe?
\end{quote}
The question matters because evaluation reports are increasingly relied
  on as evidence for paper claims, leaderboard standings, model cards,
  and governance decisions, yet a published HELM record does not
  surface, on a per-record basis, the upstream-framework identity, the
  serving-stack identity, or even the recipe-level stochasticity
  parameters that determine whether two runs of nominally the same
  recipe should agree.
The audit's first job is therefore to \emph{distinguish} layers of
  disagreement, not just to count instance-level mismatches: a
  mismatch that comes from \texttt{temperature=1} stochastic sampling
  is not the same finding as a mismatch that comes from a silent
  pandas-version-dependent reshuffle of the test set.

\subsection{Audit Pipeline and Paper-Validity Guard}
\label{sec:app-helm-pipeline-v2}

The audit consumes EEE artifacts for the public HELM corpus (the
  \texttt{crfm-helm-public-eee-test} mirror) and EEE artifacts for the
  local audit runs, joins them by logical run key, and emits per-pair
  agreement reports plus a cross-cell heatmap.
The slim-paper pipeline runs three stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \texttt{10\_link\_tree.sh} builds a symlink tree under
        \texttt{eee\_artifacts/\{official,local\}/} so a single reader
        can walk both sides as an EEE-shaped directory hierarchy.
  \item \texttt{20\_run.sh} invokes \texttt{eval-audit-from-eee} to
        compute per-pair agreement curves and aggregate-summary
        artifacts; this is the analysis step where any reproducibility
        signal is computed.
  \item \texttt{30\_heatmap.sh} renders the publication heatmap.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Runtime EEE-only paper-validity guard.}
A reviewer concerned with the \eee{} sufficiency claim will reasonably
  ask: when the audit computes the published agreement numbers, does
  the analysis code actually use only EEE artifacts, or does it
  silently fall back to HELM run-dir reads when those happen to be on
  disk?
We resolved this two ways.
At the code level, \path{EVAL_AUDIT_EEE_STRICT=1} disables a
  legacy fallback in the EEE artifact loader that would otherwise
  overwrite EEE-derived per-instance records with
  \path{per_instance_stats.json}-derived records when the latter is
  on disk; this is the behavioural guarantee.
At the runtime level, we wrap \texttt{20\_run.sh} with a small audit
  harness
  (\path{dev/oneoff/audit_eee_only_run.py}) that injects a
  \texttt{sitecustomize.py} into every child Python process via a
  \texttt{PYTHONPATH} prefix; the injected module installs a
  \texttt{sys.addaudithook} that records every \texttt{open} and
  \texttt{os.open} event to a per-PID JSONL log, and a post-exit
  classifier flags any path whose basename is one of HELM's canonical
  artifact filenames (\texttt{run\_spec.json}, \texttt{scenario.json},
  \texttt{scenario\_state.json}, \texttt{stats.json},
  \path{per_instance_stats.json}) or whose path contains
  \texttt{benchmark\_output/runs/} (HELM's run-dir signature).
On the slim-paper run, the audit harness produced
  \emph{zero} HELM-shaped opens across 88 child Python processes,
  6{,}902 unique paths, and 205{,}798 total open events.
The published agreement numbers are therefore empirically attributable
  to EEE artifacts alone, regardless of whether the HELM run dirs
  happened to be available to the analysis host.

\subsection{Scope}
\label{sec:app-helm-scope-v2}

\paragraph{Why these three model families.}
We picked three open-weight models that span the relevant variation in
  pretraining and fine-tuning style: Pythia~6.9B (raw pretrained,
  EleutherAI), Vicuna~7B v1.3 (instruction-tuned, lmsys), and
  Falcon~7B (raw pretrained, TII).
All three have HELM Classic v0.3.0 entries in the public corpus and
  reproducible HELM runs in the local audit.
The intent of the slim choice is that the three together exercise the
  case where the model is and is not instruction-tuned, and where the
  HELM-side official deployment defaults to a hosted backend versus a
  local one (Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-pythia-pattern-v2} below).

\paragraph{Why these fourteen benchmarks.}
The fourteen benchmarks
  (BoolQ, CivilComments, Entity-DataImputation, Entity-Matching, GSM,
  IMDB, LSAT-QA, MMLU, NarrativeQA, QuAC, SyntheticReasoning,
  SyntheticReasoning-Natural, TruthfulQA, WikiFact)
  are the HELM Classic core scenarios for which all three models have
  both a public-HELM run and a local audit reproduction.
They span multiple-choice (MMLU, LSAT-QA, TruthfulQA), short-answer
  (BoolQ, CivilComments, IMDB), entity tasks (DataImputation, Matching,
  WikiFact), free-form QA (NarrativeQA, QuAC), and structured
  reasoning (GSM, SyntheticReasoning, SyntheticReasoning-Natural).

\paragraph{Comparison metric.}
Per cell we compute the \emph{micro-averaged} instance-level agreement
  at \texttt{abs\_tol=0}: the fraction of (\texttt{sample\_hash},
  \texttt{metric\_id}) rows where the per-metric
  \texttt{evaluation.score} is bit-identical between the public-HELM
  and local rows, summed across all metric rows for the cell:
\[
  \mathrm{agree\_ratio}_{\mathrm{cell}}
  \;=\;
  \frac{\sum_{(s, m) \in \mathrm{cell}}
       \mathbb{1}[\,|\mathrm{score}_{\mathrm{off}}(s, m)
                  - \mathrm{score}_{\mathrm{loc}}(s, m)| < \epsilon\,]}
       {\sum_{(s, m) \in \mathrm{cell}} 1}.
\]
We report this micro-averaged ratio per cell on the heatmap, and
  break it down per metric in the supplementary panel.
The micro-average choice means a cell with multiple metrics is
  effectively weighted by per-metric row count, and \emph{any} 0=0
  agreement between the two sides on a (sample, metric) row counts as
  a match — a property that interacts with several of the failure
  modes we report below
  (Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-microavg-v2}).
We deliberately use \texttt{abs\_tol=0} as the headline tolerance:
  looser tolerances erase the per-instance flips that the audit is
  designed to detect, and presenting the headline number at relaxed
  tolerance hides the upstream effects we want the reader to see.

\subsection{Case Study A: \texorpdfstring{\texttt{entity\_matching}}{entity\_matching} pandas-merge ordering}
\label{sec:app-helm-em-v2}

The \texttt{entity\_matching} cells render as \texttt{join\_failed}
  for all three models because the official-side and local-side
  \texttt{sample\_hash} sets have zero overlap, despite both sides
  drawing from the same Abt-Buy deepmatcher dataset and despite both
  sides exposing 1000 instances with byte-equivalent
  \texttt{run\_spec.json} adapter specs.

\paragraph{Symptom.}
Per-model: 996--999 unique \texttt{sample\_hash} values on the official
  side, 996--999 on the local side, $|S_\text{off} \cap S_\text{loc}| = 0$
  for all three models.
Sample-id namespace overlap, by contrast, is $1000/1000$ at every
  rank, and split-distribution overlap is exact: both sides report
  $507$ \texttt{valid} + $493$ \texttt{test} instances.
The two sides therefore agree on \emph{which positional indices into
  the eval pool got sampled}, but disagree on \emph{which
  dataset row each index points to}.

\paragraph{Mechanism.}
The HELM downsampler at
  \path{helm/benchmark/runner.py:downsample_eval_instances}
  hardcodes \texttt{np.random.seed(0)} and calls
  \path{np.random.choice(all_eval_instances, max_eval_instances,
  replace=False)}; given a fixed pool length, the sampled index
  sequence is therefore deterministic.
Both sides have the same pool length and produce the same sampled
  indices.
What differs is the \emph{contents} of \texttt{all\_eval\_instances} at
  each index: the upstream pandas \texttt{pd.merge} sequence inside
  \path{EntityMatchingScenario.read_blocked_pairs} produces
  different row orderings under different pandas versions, even on
  byte-identical input CSVs.
HELM's \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} subsequently assigns
  \texttt{id<i>} positionally over the merged list (the docstring
  literally reads \texttt{``Note: order of instances matters.''}), so
  any upstream ordering instability silently rebinds id $\leftrightarrow$ content.
The downstream \texttt{sample\_hash}, computed by the EEE converter as
  \path{sha256(rendered_prompt + correct_reference)}, then differs
  on a per-id basis between the two pandas regimes — precisely the
  behaviour that produces the observed zero overlap.

\paragraph{Evidence chain.}
We isolated the mechanism with two stand-alone reproducers checked into
  the released code.
The first
  (\path{dev/oneoff/em_pandas_mwe.sh}) runs only the
  \texttt{pd.merge} sequence from
  \path{EntityMatchingScenario.read_blocked_pairs} against the
  byte-identical Abt-Buy deepmatcher CSVs across five
  \mbox{(pandas, numpy)} version combos, and computes a
  \texttt{full\_order\_digest} of the resulting row sequence.
The result is a sharp boundary: \texttt{pandas==2.0.3} (with either
  \texttt{numpy==1.23.5} or \texttt{numpy==1.26.4}) produces digest
  \texttt{d449ad71...}, while every \texttt{pandas==2.2.3} or higher
  combo (with \texttt{numpy} ranging from \texttt{1.26.4} through
  \texttt{2.4.4}) produces a different digest \texttt{77d68109...}.
Numpy version is irrelevant; pandas $2.0 \rightarrow 2.2$ is the
  inflection.
The second reproducer
  (\path{dev/oneoff/em_helm_mwe.sh}) runs the full HELM scenario
  pipeline end-to-end (\path{EntityMatchingScenario.get_instances}
  $\rightarrow$ \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} $\rightarrow$
  \path{downsample_eval_instances}) and diffs the resulting
  ordered \mbox{(rank, id, content\_sig)} sequence against captured
  \texttt{scenario\_state.json} files from the actual HELM runs.
The verdict matrix is in Table~\ref{tab:helm-em-mwe-v2}.

The chain is closed: under \texttt{pandas==2.3.3}, the HELM scenario
  reproduces the local audit \texttt{scenario\_state.json} byte-for-byte;
  under \texttt{pandas==2.0.3}, it reproduces the public HELM Classic
  v0.3.0 \texttt{scenario\_state.json} byte-for-byte.
The recipe, the dataset, the downsample seed, and the framework source
  are identical in both cases; only pandas changes.

\paragraph{Three independent reproducibility leaks adjacent to this
  finding.}
HELM's PR \#1475 (commit \texttt{d69a6e6}, 2023-04-19) added a
  fixed-numpy-random-state file specifically to recover the original
  \texttt{entity\_matching} sampling, and that fix is active in both
  v0.3.0 and v0.5.x.
The fix is necessary but not sufficient: it pins numpy's RNG state
  inside \texttt{get\_instances}, but the merge that determines which
  rows occupy each index sits upstream of the seed and is invisible
  to the fix.
The audit also surfaces two further leaks adjacent to the merge issue:
  \texttt{ensure\_file\_downloaded} at
  \texttt{helm/common/general.py:80} treats the existence of the
  target path as proof of correctness (the source comment reads
  \texttt{``Assume it's all good''}), so a stale or corrupted local
  cache silently shadows the remote dataset; and
  \texttt{with\_instance\_ids} binds \texttt{id<i>} to scenario-emit
  position, so any upstream ordering instability silently rebinds
  id $\leftrightarrow$ content without surfacing in the run-spec hash.

\paragraph{What \eee{} catches that HELM's id-based comparison misses.}
A consumer who joins the two sides on \texttt{(sample\_id, metric\_id)}
  would observe perfect 1000/1000 overlap and infer that the runs
  agree on which examples were evaluated.
Joining on the content-addressed \texttt{sample\_hash} (which the EEE
  converter computes as
  \path{sha256(rendered_prompt + correct_reference)}) correctly
  rejects the join, because the prompts, although under the same
  \texttt{id<i>}, refer to different concrete product pairs.
The \texttt{join\_failed} cell is therefore not a flaw in the audit: it
  is the audit correctly refusing to assert agreement on samples whose
  content disagrees.

\subsection{Case Study B: \texorpdfstring{SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia-6.9B}{SR-Natural x Pythia-6.9B} (0.788) --- broken official inference}
\label{sec:app-helm-srnat-v2}

The \texttt{SyntheticReasoning-Natural~$\times$~Pythia-6.9B} cell
  renders at $0.788$, the lowest non-\texttt{join\_failed} cell in the
  slim heatmap.
Vicuna and Falcon are at $0.998$ and $0.997$ respectively on the same
  benchmark, so the issue is Pythia-specific.
The recipe is bytewise-equivalent on both sides:
  \texttt{temperature=0.0, num\_outputs=1, max\_tokens=20,
  stop\_sequences=[`\textbackslash n']}, and
  \path{set_fixed_random_state_for_dataset} is active.
At \texttt{temperature=0} the model should be deterministic, so the
  divergence is at the inference-stack layer.

\paragraph{Symptom.}
On the public HELM side, the per-instance scoring file
  (\path{per_instance_stats.json}) records score $0.0$ on every
  one of the 1000 instances for all three set-match metrics
  (\texttt{f1\_set\_match}, \texttt{iou\_set\_match},
  \texttt{exact\_set\_match}), and the run-level mean across each
  metric is $0.000$.
Cross-checked against \texttt{scenario\_state.json}, every
  \path{request_state[i].result.completions[0].text} is the empty
  string, with \texttt{n\_tokens=20} (i.e., the model always hits
  \texttt{max\_tokens} and never emits EOS).
The EEE conversion faithfully mirrors HELM:
  \path{answer_attribution[*].extracted_value=""} for all 20{,}000
  EEE records (1000 instances $\times$ 20 metric ids), and
  \texttt{evaluation.score=0.0} for the three set-match metric ids.
Our local re-run, in contrast, produces \texttt{n\_tokens$\approx$5} on
  average, real \texttt{completion.text} values like
  \texttt{``The dog is old.''}, and run-level means around
  $0.21 / 0.21 / 0.18$.

\paragraph{Mechanism.}
HELM v0.3.0's \texttt{auto\_client.py} at lines 184--200 routes any
  model whose organization prefix is \texttt{eleutherai}, \texttt{lmsys},
  \texttt{tiiuae}, etc., \emph{when no \texttt{model\_deployment} is
  specified}, to \texttt{TogetherClient} — a hosted-inference proxy
  for Together.ai's API.
The official \texttt{run\_spec.json} for the slim-heatmap Pythia runs
  has \texttt{model\_deployment=None} on every field
  (\texttt{adapter\_spec}, \texttt{request}, top-level), so the
  official runs were inferenced via Together.ai.
Our local-audit \texttt{run\_spec.json} pins
  \path{model_deployment="huggingface/pythia-6.9b"}, so the local
  runs use HuggingFace transformers locally.
On Together's hosted Pythia at \texttt{temperature=0}, the greedy first
  token after a prompt ending in \texttt{:} is the newline token
  \texttt{`\textbackslash n'} (HELM \texttt{scenario\_state} records
  \texttt{tokens[0].text == `\d{C}'}, the BPE encoding of newline).
Tokens 2--6 are the actual model answer
  (e.g.\ \texttt{[`The', ` dog', ` is', ` sad', `.']}) and tokens 7--20
  are further newlines.
HELM's \texttt{stop\_sequences=[`\textbackslash n']} trims
  \texttt{completion.text} at the first newline, so the recorded
  \texttt{completion.text} is empty even though the underlying token
  stream contains a real answer.
On HuggingFace transformers locally, Pythia's greedy first token under
  the same prompt is \texttt{`The'} (not \texttt{`\textbackslash n'}),
  so the trim happens after a real answer is captured.

\paragraph{Independent verification.}
The published Stanford HELM Classic v0.3.0 dashboard at
  \texttt{crfm.stanford.edu/helm/classic/latest/\#/groups/synthetic\_reasoning\_natural}
  reports Pythia-6.9B at $0\%$ accuracy on
  \path{synthetic_reasoning_natural} for the three set-match
  metrics, consistent with the empty-completion mechanism documented
  here.
The published number is therefore a real artifact of the published
  audit, not a measurement of the model's capability on the task.

\paragraph{Why the $0.788$ understates the gap.}
The headline cell-level agreement of $0.788$ is a micro-average over
  $3000$ \mbox{(sample, metric)} rows; per-metric it breaks down as
  $\{0.819, 0.772, 0.772\}$.
The bigger story is that, with the official run scoring $0.0$ on every
  row, every $\text{LOCAL}=0$ outcome counts as a $0=0$ agreement, and
  the $0.788$ thus reads as ``fraction of prompts on which the local
  side also scored zero'' rather than ``fraction of cases where the
  two model behaviours agree''.
The aggregate mean per metric is $0.000$ on the official side and
  $\approx 0.20$ on the local side; the leaderboard-cited
  numbers therefore differ by the entire model accuracy, while the
  cell-level $0.788$ obscures this.
We treat this as a methodological caveat for any micro-averaged
  agreement metric and discuss it in
  Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-microavg-v2}.

\subsection{Case Study C: \texorpdfstring{\texttt{wikifact} ($\approx 0.92$ across all three models)}{wikifact (approx 0.92 across all three models)} --- stochastic noise floor}
\label{sec:app-helm-wikifact-v2}

The three \texttt{wikifact} cells agree at $0.920$, $0.922$, $0.927$
  for Vicuna, Pythia, and Falcon respectively.
The recipe (verified bytewise on both sides) is
  \texttt{temperature=1.0, num\_outputs=5, max\_tokens=8,
  stop\_sequences=[`\textbackslash n']}.
HELM uses \texttt{temperature=1.0} on \texttt{wikifact} to draw five
  diverse outputs for the \texttt{exact\_match@5} family of metrics.
Two independent runs of a stochastic recipe are not bitwise
  reproducible by construction.

For a binary metric with per-prompt hit rate $p_i$ (defined as the
  per-prompt probability that the metric scores 1 on a single
  stochastic run, i.e.,~the per-prompt \texttt{exact\_match@5} success
  probability), the expected agreement of two independent stochastic
  runs over a benchmark of $N$ prompts is
\[
  \mathbb{E}_i[\,\text{agree}(p_i)\,]
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}_i[\,p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2\,].
\]
The Lean-verified identity at the heart of our analysis (theorem
  \path{expectedAgreement_eq_uniformAgreement_add_variance})
  decomposes this as
\[
  \mathbb{E}_i[\,\text{agree}(p_i)\,]
  \;=\;
  \text{agree}(\bar p) \;+\; 2\,\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i),
\]
where $\bar p = \mathbb{E}_i[p_i]$ is the prompt-averaged hit rate and
  $\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i) = \mathbb{E}_i[(p_i - \bar p)^2]$.
The first term is the homogeneous-Bernoulli prediction one would make
  if every prompt had the same hit rate $\bar p$; the second is a
  non-negative variance term that captures prompt-level
  heterogeneity.
Together they imply (theorem
  \path{uniformAgreement_le_expectedAgreement}) that
  $\text{agree}(\bar p) \le \mathbb{E}_i[\text{agree}(p_i)]$: the
  homogeneous prediction at the mean is a lower bound on the
  heterogeneous expected agreement.

Concretely: the homogeneous prediction is
  $\text{agree}(\bar p) = 2(\bar p - \tfrac{1}{2})^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}$
  (theorem \texttt{agree\_centered}), which lies in
  $[0.5, 0.82]$ for $\bar p \in [0.5, 0.9]$.
A homogeneous model with $\bar p \in [0.85, 0.90]$ produces
  agreement in $[0.745, 0.82]$ (theorem \texttt{agree\_range\_85\_90})
  and \emph{cannot} produce $0.92$ (theorem
  \texttt{not\_agree\_92\_on\_85\_90}).
Producing a heterogeneous expected agreement of $\approx 0.92$
  therefore requires either extreme $\bar p$ ($> 0.92$ or
  $< 0.08$) or non-trivial $\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i)$ — the latter being
  the prompt-level heterogeneity one would expect for a trained model
  on a benchmark with prompt-dependent difficulty (some prompts the
  model gets consistently right, others consistently wrong).
Our observed $\approx 0.92$ across all three models is consistent with
  the latter regime, and the cross-model uniformity (Pythia at $0.922$
  vs.\ Vicuna at $0.920$ and Falcon at $0.927$) is itself diagnostic:
  Pythia is a systematic outlier on every other benchmark in the
  table, but on \texttt{wikifact} it converges to the same $\approx 0.92$
  the instruction-tuned and Falcon variants achieve, supporting the
  interpretation that recipe-induced stochasticity is the main driver
  of disagreement on this cell rather than any model-specific backend
  effect (Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-pythia-pattern-v2}).
\footnote{The supporting Bernoulli identities and bound theorems
  cited above are formalized in
  \path{dev/paper/wikifact_consistency_claim.lean}, which
  compiles against \texttt{Mathlib} and is verified independently of
  this manuscript.}

\paragraph{Note on the 1-vs-5 EEE \texttt{output.raw} difference.}
On the public HELM side, EEE's \texttt{output.raw} is a 1-element list
  (e.g.\ \texttt{["New York City"]}); on the local side it is a
  5-element list (e.g.\ \texttt{["New York City", "Newark", "Albany",
  "Brooklyn", "Manhattan"]}).
This is a converter cosmetic, not a HELM-side recipe difference: HELM
  computed the \texttt{*@5} metrics with all 5 outputs at run time and
  stored the resulting per-metric scores, which are bitwise-equivalent
  in interpretation.
The older \texttt{every\_eval\_ever} converter active when the public
  EEE store was produced truncated \texttt{output.raw} to the first
  generation for display; the current converter retains all 5.
The diversity of the local \texttt{output.raw} list is itself the
  evidence that \texttt{temperature>0} sampling was active --- a
  helpful side-effect of preserving the full output set, since EEE's
  \texttt{metadata} field does not record temperature explicitly.

  at \texttt{temperature=0}, the greedy first token is sensitive to
  small differences in the inference stack (precision, attention
  kernel, tokenizer interaction), and Together-hosted Pythia and
  HuggingFace-served Pythia disagree at the first token on a fraction
  of prompts.
On SR-Natural the disagreement happens to interact catastrophically
  with HELM's stop-sequence trimming
  (Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-srnat-v2}); on the other seven cells the
  disagreement produces different but valid model outputs.

  which agree at $\geq 0.984$ on every non-WikiFact cell despite also
  being Together-hosted in the public HELM corpus.

\paragraph{A logit-margin hypothesis for the Pythia sensitivity.}
We hypothesize that Pythia-6.9B's higher inference-stack sensitivity
  reflects \emph{narrower per-token logit margins}.
The mechanism: when one inference stack serves at \texttt{fp16} or
  \texttt{bf16} and another at \texttt{fp32}, the resulting logit
  perturbations between the two stacks are typically on the order of
  $10^{-4}$ in the body of the next-token distribution.
That perturbation flips the greedy argmax only when the top-1 and
  top-2 logits at the relevant decoding position are within
  $\sim 10^{-4}$ of each other.
How often that happens depends on \emph{how peaked} the model's
  next-token distribution is at the position in question, which in
  turn depends on training compute and post-pretraining alignment.
Table~\ref{tab:helm-pythia-factors-v2} summarises the relevant
  axes for the three slim-heatmap models.

  Falcon share the GPT-NeoX BPE tokenizer but show very different
  sensitivities), and attention architecture is too entangled with
  training-time effects to isolate from this evidence.

\paragraph{What we cannot rule out from three-model evidence.}
The logit-margin hypothesis above is consistent with our observations
  but not proven by them.
Confounds we cannot rule out without further experiments:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Different served checkpoint.}
        Together may host a Pythia-6.9B revision or quantization that
        differs from the HuggingFace Hub checkpoint our local audit
        uses, in which case the divergence is partly a ``wrong
        weights'' effect rather than a numerical-precision effect.
        We did not directly compare the served weights.
  \item \textbf{Different serving precision.}
        We do not know the exact serving precision Together used for
        Pythia-6.9B in the historical HELM Classic v0.3.0 runs.
        Different precisions (\texttt{fp16}, \texttt{bf16}, \texttt{int8})
        produce different perturbation magnitudes; \texttt{int8} in
        particular would explain a much higher sensitivity than
        \texttt{fp16}.
  \item \textbf{Architecture-specific numerical sensitivity.}
        Falcon's multi-query attention reduces the number of KV-heads
        and is empirically more numerically stable under \texttt{fp16}
        serving than standard multi-head attention.
        Some of Falcon's apparent insensitivity may be the architecture
        rather than the training compute or alignment.
  \item \textbf{Generation-kernel differences.}
        Together's hosted-inference stack and HuggingFace's
        \texttt{transformers} use different fused-kernel paths for
        attention and softmax; differences in those paths can produce
        small per-position logit drift independently of the model's
        precision.
\end{itemize}
Distinguishing among these would require A/B testing against
  Together's current API at known precision, which we have not done.
Such an experiment would not change the case-study claim — that the
  divergence exists, is detected by EEE's \texttt{sample\_hash}, is
  consistent with a backend-substitution effect, and disproportionately
  affects the model with the smallest training-compute budget and no
  instruction tuning — but would localize the proximate cause to one of
  the four candidates above.

\subsection{EEE audit/forensics scope}
\label{sec:app-helm-eee-scope-v2}

EEE is sufficient for audit: detecting whether two runs agree, by how
  much, on which instances, and at what cell-level granularity.
EEE is not sufficient for forensics: identifying \emph{why} the
  disagreement occurred typically requires the upstream framework's
  run-time artifacts.
Each of the three case studies above made this audit/forensics split
  visible in concrete terms: Case~A's pandas-merge mechanism required
  inspecting HELM's \texttt{run\_spec.json} adapter spec, the
  \texttt{scenario\_state.json} per-instance ordering, and the
  \path{entity_matching_scenario.py} source; Case~B's
  Together-hosted-Pythia mechanism required HELM's
  \texttt{auto\_client.py} dispatch logic; Case~C's noise-floor
  diagnosis required the \texttt{run\_spec.json} \texttt{temperature}
  field.
EEE preserved the divergence signal on all three; it did not preserve
  the upstream framework version, the inference backend identity, or
  the precision/kernel of the generation step.

This separation is a deliberate scope decision.
The audit layer is designed to be small, framework-neutral, and stable
  across harness versions; the forensics layer is necessarily
  framework-specific (different harnesses pin recipe in different
  ways) and benefits from being held separate so the audit format does
  not have to track every harness's evolution.
A version of \eee{} that preserved the framework run-time artifacts
  would replicate the very provenance fragility the case studies
  surface: the \texttt{run\_spec.json} schema evolves across HELM
  releases, the \texttt{auto\_client.py} dispatch table changes, and
  the field that determines deployment routing today may not exist a
  year from now.
The audit/forensics split protects \eee{} consumers from that
  evolution: an EEE artifact computed today remains parsable and
  auditable by a consumer five years from now even if HELM v1.x has
  reorganized its source tree.

The accompanying scope-distinction table is documented in
  \texttt{docs/eee-vs-helm-metadata.md} alongside the per-question
  classification.

\subsection{Methodological caveats: micro-averaged agree\_ratio}
\label{sec:app-helm-microavg-v2}

The headline cell-level agree\_ratio is a micro-average over
  \mbox{(sample, metric)} rows: the cell value is $\sum
  \text{matched} / \sum \text{count}$ across all rows, weighting each
  row equally.
This is the right summary statistic for a reproducibility heatmap
  whose readers want to know ``what fraction of per-instance
  per-metric scores agree?''
But three failure modes of this micro-average are visible in the slim
  heatmap and worth surfacing for any reader of a reproducibility
  report; all three flow from the same property: any $0=0$ (or
  identical-score) collision between the two sides counts as a match,
  even when the underlying outputs differ.

\paragraph{Degenerate-zero agreement (catastrophic case).}
When one side's run is degenerate
  (e.g., the SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia broken official run scoring
  $0$ on every row), every \mbox{(sample, metric)} row where the
  other side also scores $0$ counts as agreement at \texttt{abs\_tol=0}.
The agreement rate then reads as ``fraction of prompts on which the
  non-degenerate side also failed'', not ``fraction of cases where
  the two model behaviours agree''.
SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia at $0.788$ is this: every ``agreement''
  is a $0=0$ collision on a hard prompt, while the underlying
  run-level means are $0.000$ official and $\approx 0.20$ local.
The cell-level number is honest at the per-row level but misleads
  at the leaderboard level if read in isolation.

\paragraph{Both-wrong masking (mild case).}
The same mechanism appears in milder form on cells where the model
  is moderately bad at the benchmark.
On SR~$\times$~Falcon-7B (cell agree\_ratio $0.999$), $10$ of $1000$
  prompts have bytewise-different \texttt{output.raw} between Together
  and HF — a $1.0\%$ true model-output divergence rate.
But of those $10$ output-divergent prompts, only $3$ produce score
  disagreements across the four core metrics
  (\texttt{exact\_match}, \texttt{prefix\_exact\_match},
  \texttt{quasi\_exact\_match}, \path{quasi_prefix_exact_match})
  $\times$ $1000$ prompts $=$ $4000$ score rows, because the other $7$
  output-divergent prompts produce outputs that both miss the gold
  reference, so the score is $0=0$ on every metric and the row counts
  as ``agreement''.
The cell-level number $0.9992$ thus hides a $\sim$10$\times$ larger
  output-divergence rate.
This is not a flaw in the metric — it accurately reports score
  agreement — but a reader who interprets the cell as ``the models
  emit nearly identical outputs'' is wrong by an order of magnitude.

\paragraph{Stochastic noise floor.}
For recipes with \texttt{temperature>0}, two independent runs are not
  bitwise reproducible by design.
The expected agreement under a per-prompt Bernoulli model with
  per-prompt hit rate $p_i$ (the per-prompt
  \texttt{exact\_match@k} probability) decomposes exactly as
  $\mathbb{E}_i[\,\text{agree}(p_i)\,] = \text{agree}(\bar p) + 2\,
  \mathrm{Var}_i(p_i)$
  (Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-wikifact-v2}), with the homogeneous
  prediction $\text{agree}(\bar p) = \bar p^2 + (1-\bar p)^2$ a lower
  bound on the heterogeneous expectation.
\texttt{wikifact} at $\approx 0.92$ across all three models is
  consistent with prompt-level heterogeneity in $p_i$ — many prompts
  near-deterministic, which is what one would expect for a trained
  model on a benchmark with prompt-dependent difficulty.
A reviewer who interprets the cell as ``92\% reproducibility'' rather
  than ``stochastic recipe at the heterogeneity-corrected expected
  agreement'' is missing the recipe-level non-determinism the
  benchmark explicitly enables.

\paragraph{What honest reporting looks like.}
A reproducibility report that surfaces \texttt{agree\_ratio} alone
  cannot distinguish the three cases above from a recipe-clean
  near-perfect reproduction at $0.92$ or $0.999$.
Honest reporting wants to surface, alongside \texttt{agree\_ratio},
  both the run-level mean per metric on each side (to expose the
  degenerate-zero case), the recipe's \texttt{temperature} setting
  (to expose the stochastic-floor case), and the
  output-divergence rate
  ($\Pr_i[\,\texttt{output.raw}_{\mathrm{off}}^{(i)} \neq
  \texttt{output.raw}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(i)}\,]$, to expose the
  both-wrong-masking case).
The pipeline released alongside this paper emits the first two
  alongside the cell-level number; a future iteration of the heatmap
  visualization could surface all three inline, e.g., as a per-cell
  tuple of (micro-averaged agree\_ratio, official mean, local mean,
  output-divergence rate), so a reader can flag cells where the
  agreement reading is misleading without leaving the figure.

\subsection{What this audit does not measure}
\label{sec:app-helm-limits-v2}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Closed-API and hosted-only models.}
        The audit covers only open-weight models for which a local
        re-execution is meaningful; we did not attempt to audit
        Together-hosted-only or API-only models.
  \item \textbf{Multi-turn and agentic evaluations.}
        HELM's multi-turn and agent-tool scenario classes introduce
        additional provenance layers (agent scaffold version, tool
        registry, sandbox snapshot) not exercised here.
  \item \textbf{Cross-harness alignment.}
        The audit does not align HELM scores against
        \texttt{lm-eval-harness} or \texttt{Inspect-AI} scores for
        the same model and benchmark, which would introduce a second
        axis of harness-level provenance drift.
  \item \textbf{Together-side direct verification of Case~B.}
        We have direct evidence that the public-side Pythia run was
        Together-routed (HELM v0.3.0 \texttt{auto\_client.py} dispatch
        logic) and that its outputs match the empty-completion
        pattern, but we did not call Together's API directly to
        verify that today's Together-hosted Pythia reproduces the
        2023-era empty-completion behaviour.
        This is a useful follow-on if a reviewer pushes back on the
        Case~B mechanism, but is not strictly required for the
        audit-level claim that the public HELM Classic v0.3.0
        SR-Natural~$\times$~Pythia score is an artifact rather than a
        capability measurement.
  \item \textbf{Quantification of the systematic Pythia-Together pattern.}
        Section~\ref{sec:app-helm-pythia-pattern-v2} characterizes
        the eight Pythia outlier cells qualitatively, but does not
        produce a per-cell quantification of how much of each
        agreement gap is attributable to backend substitution versus
        any other effect.
        A more thorough version of that section would re-run each
        Pythia cell on a HuggingFace-deployment-only public-side mirror
        (if one were available) and quantify the residual; we do not
        do this for the present paper.
\end{itemize}

Stage 1 (link tree) takes seconds.
Stage 2 (analysis under audit hook) takes 2--4 minutes wall-clock on
  a single host, dominated by EEE-side per-pair report rendering.
Stage 3 (heatmap render) takes seconds.
