\section{HELM Reproducibility Audit Details}
\label{sec:app-helm-audit}

This appendix gives the technical evidence for
Section~\ref{sec:cs3-helm}.  The goal is to keep the paper-level case study
focused on the scientific point---\eee{} separates invalid comparisons,
diagnostic discrepancies, and expected stochastic disagreement---while retaining
enough detail for a reader to evaluate the audit.

\subsection{Audit scope and agreement metric}
\label{sec:app-helm-scope}

We compared public HELM records against local re-executions for three
open-weight models (Pythia-6.9B, Vicuna-7B v1.3, Falcon-7B) on fourteen
single-turn benchmarks drawn from the relevant HELM suites for each model
(including HELM Classic where applicable): BoolQ, CivilComments, Entity-DataImputation,
Entity-Matching, GSM, IMDB, LSAT-QA, MMLU, NarrativeQA, QuAC,
SyntheticReasoning, SyntheticReasoning-Natural, TruthfulQA, and WikiFact.  The
comparison covers 42 \mbox{(model, benchmark)} cells.  Both sides are converted
to \eee{} aggregate and instance-level records.  We align instances by
\texttt{sample\_hash}, the SHA-256 hash of the rendered prompt and reference,
and compute the per-cell agreement as the fraction of aligned instances on
which the official and local scores match within \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9},
micro-averaged over instances and over the benchmark's canonical core-metric
set (rather than over every recorded metric).

This metric is intentionally stricter than comparing aggregate scores.  It asks
whether the same prompt-reference pair received the same metric value on each
side.  It also intentionally refuses to produce a number when the prompt sets do
not overlap.  In the heatmap, 39 cells have aligned instances and three cells
are \texttt{join\_failed}; the failed joins are exactly the three
Entity-Matching cells.

\subsection{Entity-Matching: identical ids, different examples}
\label{sec:app-helm-entity-matching}

The Entity-Matching result is a non-comparability failure, not a model failure.
The public and local records each contain 1,000 Abt--Buy examples, and HELM's
positional identifiers overlap 1,000/1,000.  Nevertheless, the
\texttt{sample\_hash} sets have zero overlap.  Inspecting the upstream scenario
construction isolates the cause: \texttt{EntityMatchingScenario} forms a merged
candidate table with \texttt{pd.merge}; HELM then applies a fixed-seed
\texttt{np.random.seed(0)} downsample and assigns ids positionally.  A version
change in pandas changes the order of the merged table, so the fixed downsample
selects different product pairs even though the input CSVs, seed, and HELM
recipe are the same.

Two reproducer checks close the loop.  First, a pandas-only reproducer applies
the exact merge sequence to the Abt--Buy CSVs and hashes the resulting row order:
pandas 2.0.3 yields one stable digest, while pandas 2.2.3 and newer yield a
different stable digest.  Sweeping numpy versions while holding pandas fixed
does not change the digest.  Second, an end-to-end HELM-scenario reproducer
shows that the current environment reproduces the local
\texttt{scenario\_state.json} byte-for-byte, whereas a HELM v0.3.0-era
environment reproduces the public \texttt{scenario\_state.json} byte-for-byte
(Table~\ref{tab:app-helm-em-mwe}).  The only load-bearing difference is the
pandas regime.

because the underlying prompt-reference content is different.

\subsection{SyntheticReasoning-Natural: an empty-completion artifact}
\label{sec:app-helm-sr-natural}

The SyntheticReasoning-Natural $\times$ Pythia-6.9B cell is the largest
post-join discrepancy.  Unlike Entity-Matching, the prompt-reference hashes
match: the two sides are evaluating the same instances.  The discrepancy appears
after alignment.  The public-side records have \texttt{output.raw=""} and score
0.0 on every instance for the set-match metrics; the local records have
non-empty completions and recover non-zero accuracy.

The HELM recipe is deterministic on paper:
\texttt{temperature=0.0}, \texttt{num\_outputs=1}, \texttt{max\_tokens=20}, and
\texttt{stop\_sequences=[``\textbackslash n'']}.  The audit trail indicates that
HELM v0.3.0 routed the public Pythia call through Together.ai when
\texttt{model\_deployment} was unset, while the local run used HuggingFace
transformers.  In the public artifacts, the first generated token is a newline
and the following tokens contain the substantive answer.  HELM applies the
newline stop sequence before scoring, so the recorded completion becomes empty.
The local HuggingFace run does not start with a newline and therefore preserves
the answer.

This example illustrates the boundary between audit and forensics.  \eee{}

same reference, empty official output, non-empty local output, divergent scores.
The historical backend routing is not part of the current \eee{} record and must
be recovered from HELM runtime artifacts or source code.

\subsection{WikiFact: expected agreement under stochastic decoding}
\label{sec:app-helm-wikifact}

WikiFact behaves differently from the previous two cases.  The three WikiFact
cells have similar agreement for all models: 0.922 for Pythia, 0.920 for
Vicuna, and 0.927 for Falcon.  The recipe uses \texttt{temperature=1.0},
\texttt{num\_outputs=5}, \texttt{max\_tokens=8}, and a newline stop sequence.
Therefore independent executions of the same recipe are not expected to be
bitwise identical.

A simple Bernoulli model explains why agreement can stabilize below one without
indicating a reproducibility bug.  Let $p_i$ be the probability that a single
stochastic execution scores 1 on prompt $i$.  The probability that two
independent executions agree on that prompt is
\[
  p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2.
\]
Averaging over prompts gives
\[
  \mathbb{E}_i[p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2]
  = \bar p^2 + (1-\bar p)^2 + 2\,\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i),
\]
where $\bar p=\mathbb{E}_i[p_i]$.  The homogeneous Bernoulli agreement
$\bar p^2 + (1-\bar p)^2$ is therefore a lower bound on the heterogeneous
expected agreement.  The observed WikiFact values sit slightly above this lower
bound at the corresponding run-level accuracies, which is consistent with
prompt-dependent difficulty: some prompts are almost always answered correctly,
some almost always incorrectly, and some are genuinely variable.

The \eee{} fields are sufficient to make this interpretation visible.  The
records preserve the non-zero temperature, the number of sampled completions,
the per-instance scores, and the raw output structure.  A scalar leaderboard
score alone would obscure the distinction between stochastic recipe behavior and
a broken reproduction.

\subsection{Residual sub-unit agreement and the Pythia pattern}
\label{sec:app-helm-pythia-pattern}

Several additional cells fall below perfect agreement but do not exhibit the
Entity-Matching or empty-completion failure modes.  Spot checks show that the
same instances are being evaluated and that most answers agree; the residual
disagreements are genuine answer differences on a minority of prompts.  These
cells are concentrated in Pythia-6.9B, whereas Vicuna and Falcon are nearly
identical on the same non-WikiFact tasks.

The most plausible explanation is serving-stack sensitivity.  The public runs
and local runs use different inference stacks, and small numerical differences
can flip greedy decoding when the top two next-token logits are close.  Pythia
may be more sensitive because it is a weaker base model than the instruction-tuned
Vicuna and the more heavily trained Falcon model; however, this is only a
hypothesis.  The present evidence cannot rule out a different served checkpoint,
quantization, precision, tokenizer implementation, or generation kernel.
Accordingly, the paper should phrase this as a lead generated by the \eee{}
audit rather than as a causal conclusion.

\subsection{Methodological caveat: agreement is not behavioral identity}
\label{sec:app-helm-microaverage}

The heatmap reports micro-averaged agreement over per-instance metric rows.  A
row counts as agreement whenever the scores match, including when both sides are
wrong.  Consequently, high agreement does not imply identical text outputs, and
low agreement does not always mean that the evaluation was invalid.  The three
main cases occupy different regimes: Entity-Matching is an invalid comparison
because the content sets do not overlap; SyntheticReasoning-Natural with Pythia
is a valid content join with a broken official completion pattern; and WikiFact
is a valid content join whose non-unit agreement is expected from stochastic
decoding.  A reproducibility report should therefore present agreement together
with run-level scores, decoding parameters, and, when available, output-level
divergence.

\subsection{What EEE captures}
\label{sec:app-helm-eee-scope}

This case study should be read as an audit of evaluation records, not as a claim
that \eee{} replaces the original framework logs.  \eee{} captures the fields
needed to detect and localize discrepancies: content-derived instance identity,
per-instance outputs, per-metric scores, and generation parameters.  It does not
currently capture all runtime facts needed for complete forensics, such as the
exact pandas version, inference backend, served checkpoint revision, numerical
precision, or generation kernel.  The resulting division of labor is appropriate:
\eee{} makes invalid or suspicious comparisons visible at scale, while targeted
framework artifacts explain the mechanism once a suspicious cell has been found.
