\section{HELM Reproducibility Audit Details}
\label{sec:app-helm-audit}

This appendix gives the technical evidence for Section~\ref{sec:cs3-helm}. We
compare HELM-released records, called \emph{official}, to local re-executions
for Pythia-6.9B, Vicuna-7B v1.3, and Falcon-7B on fourteen single-turn HELM
benchmarks. Both sides are converted to \eee{} and aligned by
\texttt{sample\_hash}, the SHA-256 hash of the rendered prompt and reference.
Each heatmap cell reports the fraction of aligned \mbox{(instance, core metric)}
score pairs that match within \texttt{abs\_tol=1e-9}. Of the 42
\mbox{(model, benchmark)} cells, 39 align and three fail to join; all three
failures are Entity-Matching.

\subsection{Entity-Matching}
\label{sec:app-helm-entity-matching}

Entity-Matching is a non-comparability failure. Official and local records each
contain 1,000 Abt--Buy examples, and HELM positional identifiers overlap
1,000/1,000, but the \texttt{sample\_hash} sets have zero overlap.
\texttt{EntityMatchingScenario} constructs a candidate table with
\texttt{pd.merge}, then applies fixed-seed sampling and assigns ids
positionally. A pandas row-order change permutes the candidate table before
sampling, so the same input files, seed, and HELM recipe select different
product pairs.

End-to-end reproduction confirms the split: the audit environment
(pandas 2.3.3, numpy 2.2.6) exactly matches the local
\texttt{scenario\_state.json} and has 0/1000 content matches with the official
capture, while the HELM v0.3.0-era environment (pandas 2.0.3, numpy 1.23.5)
exactly matches the official capture and has 0/1000 content matches with the
local capture. Thus an id-based join would align different prompts, while the
\eee{} content join correctly reports \texttt{join\_failed}.

\subsection{SyntheticReasoning-Natural}
\label{sec:app-helm-sr-natural}

SyntheticReasoning-Natural $\times$ Pythia-6.9B is the largest post-join
discrepancy. The prompt-reference hashes match, but official records have
\texttt{output.raw=""} and score 0.0 on every instance under the set-match
metrics, while local records contain non-empty completions and recover non-zero
accuracy.

The recipe is deterministic on paper:
\texttt{temperature=0.0}, \texttt{num\_outputs=1}, \texttt{max\_tokens=20}, and
\texttt{stop\_sequences=[``\textbackslash n'']}. Lower-level official artifacts
show that the model produced outputs, but each output began with the newline
stop sequence and HELM applied that stop sequence before scoring. The audit
trail indicates that HELM v0.3.0 routed the official Pythia call through
Together.ai when \texttt{model\_deployment} was unset, whereas the local run
used Hugging Face transformers. Thus, \eee{} exposes the decisive symptom---same
prompt, same reference, empty official output, non-empty local output,
divergent scores---but the historical backend must be recovered from HELM
runtime artifacts or source code.

\subsection{WikiFact}
\label{sec:app-helm-wikifact}

WikiFact has similar agreement for all models: 0.922 for Pythia, 0.920 for
Vicuna, and 0.927 for Falcon. Its recipe uses \texttt{temperature=1.0},
\texttt{num\_outputs=5}, \texttt{max\_tokens=8}, and a newline stop sequence, so
independent executions need not match bitwise.

A simple independent Bernoulli model for binary per-instance scores explains
why agreement can stabilize below one without indicating a bug. Let $p_i$ be
the probability that one stochastic execution scores 1 on prompt $i$. Then two
independent executions agree with probability $p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2$, and
\[
  \mathbb{E}_i[p_i^2 + (1-p_i)^2]
  = \bar p^2 + (1-\bar p)^2 + 2\,\mathrm{Var}_i(p_i).
\]
Thus the homogeneous Bernoulli agreement
$\bar p^2 + (1-\bar p)^2$ is a lower bound on heterogeneous expected agreement.
The observed WikiFact values sit slightly above this bound, consistent with
prompt-dependent difficulty rather than a reproduction bug. \eee{} makes this
visible by preserving decoding parameters, per-instance scores, and raw outputs.

\subsection{Residual disagreements and scope}
\label{sec:app-helm-residual}

Several other cells fall below perfect agreement without exhibiting
non-comparability, empty completions, or stochastic decoding. Spot checks show
aligned instances and mostly matching answers; the residual differences are
genuine answer changes on a minority of prompts, concentrated in Pythia-6.9B. A
plausible lead is serving-stack sensitivity, but the evidence cannot separate
model weakness from served checkpoint, quantization, precision, tokenizer, or
generation-kernel differences.

The heatmap is an audit of evaluation records, not proof of behavioral identity.
A row agrees whenever metric scores match, including when both sides are wrong;
high agreement does not imply identical text outputs, and low agreement does not
necessarily imply invalidity. \eee{} captures content-derived instance identity,
per-instance outputs, per-metric scores, and generation parameters. It makes
invalid or suspicious comparisons visible at scale, while targeted framework
artifacts explain the mechanism.
